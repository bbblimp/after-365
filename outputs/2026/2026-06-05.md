## 1. The United States sanctions four International Criminal Court judges

**Original date:** 2025-06-05
**Type:** International Justice / Sanctions / Rule of Law
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-05, the Trump administration sanctioned four International Criminal Court judges: Solomy Balungi Bossa of Uganda, Luz del Carmen Ibanez Carranza of Peru, Reine Adelaide Sophie Alapini Gansou of Benin, and Beti Hohler of Slovenia. U.S. officials tied the designations to ICC actions involving Israel and earlier Afghanistan-related proceedings involving alleged U.S. conduct.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June designations look like an escalation point rather than a discrete protest. Later reporting and civil-society monitoring described the sanctions campaign as expanding beyond the prosecutor to additional judges, deputy prosecutors, Palestinian human-rights organizations, and related international officials. The court continued to function, but the sanctions created personal, financial, technology, and diplomatic pressure around ordinary judicial work.

### Direct impacts
The sanctions blocked the four judges' U.S.-linked property interests, restricted U.S.-connected dealings with them, and signaled that Washington would use economic sanctions against sitting international judges for court acts it viewed as illegitimate.

### Indirect impacts
The designations increased pressure on ICC member states to defend court independence and forced the court and supporting organizations to treat U.S.-controlled financial and technology infrastructure as an operational vulnerability.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage captured the unusual nature of sanctioning judges, but it could not yet show whether the measure would remain symbolic or become part of a broader pressure campaign. By 2026, follow-up analysis treated it as one step in a wider effort to constrain international accountability work.

### One-year assessment
The June 5 sanctions were consequential because they moved a dispute over ICC jurisdiction into personal sanctions against judges. One year later, the principal effect is institutional: the court's legal work continued, but the cost and risk of supporting it increased.

## 2. Burundi holds parliamentary elections that give the ruling party every elected seat

**Original date:** 2025-06-05
**Type:** Elections / Dominant-Party Rule / Democratic Institutions
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-05, Burundi held legislative and communal elections. The ruling CNDD-FDD later won all 100 directly elected National Assembly seats with 96.5 percent of the vote, while the main opposition CNL was barred from contesting and no other party crossed the representation threshold.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the election stands as a consolidation of dominant-party control. International IDEA recorded that the Constitutional Council confirmed the final results on 2025-06-20 and that the new 111-member legislature included co-opted seats to meet ethnic and gender requirements under the Arusha settlement. That preserved formal representational quotas, but not elected opposition representation.

### Direct impacts
The vote produced a National Assembly in which every directly elected seat was held by the ruling party, leaving opposition parties outside elected parliamentary representation.

### Indirect impacts
The result narrowed institutional checks inside parliament and increased the importance of non-parliamentary actors, courts, religious institutions, civil society, and regional observers in documenting political competition and irregularities.

### What original coverage missed
Election-day accounts could describe turnout and the voting process, but the deeper significance depended on final seat allocation, the exclusion of CNL, post-election validation, and later assessments of reported irregularities.

### One-year assessment
The June 5 election was consequential because it converted an already constrained political field into a legislature with no elected opposition seats. One year later, its importance lies less in a sudden rupture than in the formalization of dominant-party rule inside Burundi's central elected institution.

## 3. The World Bank resumes new financing to Uganda after an anti-LGBT law freeze

**Original date:** 2025-06-05
**Type:** Development Finance / Human Rights Conditionality / LGBT Rights
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-05, the World Bank resumed new financing operations in Uganda after a freeze that followed Uganda's 2023 Anti-Homosexuality Act. Reporting at the time said the move followed approval of three projects in social protection, education, and forced-displacement management, with the Bank citing mitigation measures intended to reduce harm from the law.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the decision has clearly reopened World Bank project finance rather than changing the underlying law. In March 2026, the Bank approved a six-year, $540 million urban infrastructure program for Uganda expected to benefit 5.6 million people, including 600,000 refugees. Rights groups continued to object that mitigation measures did not remove the law's risks for LGBT Ugandans.

### Direct impacts
The decision ended the freeze on new World Bank financing and allowed new project approvals to move forward despite the anti-LGBT law remaining in force.

### Indirect impacts
It created a precedent for substituting project-level safeguards for a full policy reversal when a borrower state's domestic law conflicts with donor human-rights standards.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports could identify the resumed lending and the Bank's safeguard rationale, but they could not yet show whether the resumption would become a broad reopening of the Uganda portfolio or remain limited to a small set of projects.

### One-year assessment
The June 5 resumption was consequential because it marked the practical end of a major development-finance sanction without repeal of the law that triggered it. One year later, the outcome is mixed: financing has resumed at scale, while the human-rights concern that caused the freeze remains unresolved.

## 4. Nintendo releases the Switch 2 worldwide

**Original date:** 2025-06-05
**Type:** Consumer Technology / Video Games / Platform Economics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-05, Nintendo released the Switch 2 in Japan, North America, Europe, Australia, and parts of Asia. The system launched as the successor to the 2017 Switch, with a larger screen, magnetic Joy-Con 2 controllers, mouse-style controller use, GameChat, backward compatibility for many Switch titles, and a launch bundle built around Mario Kart World.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the launch has become a major installed-base transition. Nintendo told investors in May 2026 that Switch 2 sales reached 19.86 million units by the end of the fiscal year ended March 2026, above both its initial 15 million forecast and later 19 million forecast. Nintendo said the first-year result was unusually high for its hardware launches and was helped by production expansion, pre-launch experience events, backward compatibility, and continuing software releases.

### Direct impacts
The release opened a new Nintendo hardware generation, gave publishers a new target platform, and moved Nintendo's core business from late-cycle Switch management into a new installed-base buildout.

### Indirect impacts
The successful launch supported demand for compatible software, accessories, online services, and third-party development, while also showing that backward compatibility and supply planning could reduce friction in a console transition.

### What original coverage missed
Launch-day coverage could capture pricing, features, demand, and queues, but it could not yet show whether the system would avoid the weak early supply and software gaps that often complicate hardware transitions.

### One-year assessment
The June 5 launch was consequential because it quickly established a large new platform rather than merely refreshing a device line. One year later, the evidence points to a strong transition, though the longer test remains whether software momentum can sustain the installed base beyond the launch year.
