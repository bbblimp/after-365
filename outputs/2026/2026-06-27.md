## 1. Congo and Rwanda sign a U.S.-mediated peace agreement

**Original date:** 2025-06-27
**Type:** Conflict Diplomacy / Central Africa / Critical Minerals
**Confidence:** High

### Then
The Democratic Republic of Congo and Rwanda signed a U.S.-mediated peace agreement in Washington, with Qatar also involved in the diplomatic track. The agreement addressed territorial integrity, cessation of hostilities, non-state armed groups, and an economic framework linked in part to critical-minerals investment.

### State after 365 days
One year later, the accord had produced process but not a settled peace. Later Doha and Washington steps created additional frameworks, yet fighting in eastern Congo continued, M23 remained central to the conflict, and U.S. sanctions in 2026 over Rwanda-linked mineral flows underscored the gap between the agreement and conditions on the ground.

### Direct impacts
The agreement restored a high-level diplomatic channel between Kinshasa and Kigali and gave Washington a larger role in both security mediation and mineral-sector diplomacy. It also framed future implementation around Rwandan withdrawal, action against the FDLR, and negotiations involving armed groups.

### Indirect impacts
The mineral language made the deal part of a wider competition over critical supply chains. That helped attract international attention, but it also made some analysts question whether economic access was being treated as a substitute for the harder security and accountability issues in eastern Congo.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage correctly noted the absence of M23 from the bilateral agreement, but the scale of that omission became clearer over time. Continued clashes and later sanctions showed that state-to-state signatures did not by themselves control armed actors or illicit mineral channels.

### One-year assessment
The agreement was consequential as a diplomatic framework, not as a completed peace. Its archival significance lies in the way it joined conflict mediation, U.S. strategic minerals policy, and regional security, while leaving the core armed conflict unresolved.

## 2. The Supreme Court limits universal injunctions in Trump v. CASA

**Original date:** 2025-06-27
**Type:** Judicial Power / Executive Authority / Immigration Law
**Confidence:** High

### Then
The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that lower federal courts generally may not issue universal injunctions that block federal policy nationwide for nonparties. The case arose from challenges to President Donald Trump's executive order seeking to restrict birthright citizenship, but the Court did not decide the citizenship question itself.

### State after 365 days
The ruling immediately shifted litigation strategy. Challengers pursued class-wide relief, a New Hampshire federal judge later certified a class and blocked enforcement for covered children and parents, and the underlying birthright-citizenship dispute returned to the Supreme Court for merits review in 2026.

### Direct impacts
The decision narrowed one common tool used to halt national executive policies at the district-court level. It also forced plaintiffs to rely more heavily on class actions, state standing theories, Administrative Procedure Act claims, or narrower injunctions tied to named parties.

### Indirect impacts
Because the decision concerned remedies rather than the substantive constitutional issue, it created legal uncertainty before creating a final rule for birthright citizenship. The practical result was not an immediate nationwide implementation of the executive order, but a more procedural and fragmented path for challenges to executive action.

### What original coverage missed
The first-day framing often treated the decision as a broad presidential victory. The following year showed a more complicated picture: the administration gained a real procedural advantage, but class actions and state litigation preserved broad relief in some settings.

### One-year assessment
Trump v. CASA mattered less as a birthright-citizenship ruling than as a durable change to federal remedies. Its consequence after a year was visible in the migration of high-stakes litigation away from universal injunctions and toward more technical vehicles for nationwide or class-wide relief.

## 3. The United States and China announce a rare-earth trade de-escalation

**Original date:** 2025-06-27
**Type:** Trade / Critical Minerals / Industrial Policy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
U.S. officials said Washington and Beijing had reached an agreement intended to ease China's export of rare-earth magnets and minerals to American firms. The announcement followed tariffs, Chinese export restrictions, and negotiations in Geneva and London.

### State after 365 days
The deal eased immediate pressure but did not remove rare earths from the center of U.S.-China competition. By 2026, licensing controls, retaliatory restrictions, and U.S. efforts to build non-Chinese magnet supply all remained active, showing that the June 2025 agreement was a tactical pause rather than a structural settlement.

### Direct impacts
The agreement helped reopen or speed some magnet and rare-earth flows to U.S. manufacturers and was treated by officials as evidence that tariff pressure and talks could produce concessions. It also confirmed that China's export-permit system had become a central bargaining tool.

### Indirect impacts
The episode accelerated policy and commercial attention to rare-earth supply chains, including domestic and allied magnet production. It also linked trade talks, defense procurement, electric vehicles, semiconductors, and industrial resilience more tightly than ordinary tariff disputes.

### What original coverage missed
The immediate coverage emphasized whether magnets would resume flowing. The larger issue was that Beijing had demonstrated a repeatable chokepoint strategy, while the United States still lacked enough processing and magnet capacity to treat rare-earth independence as a near-term fix.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 agreement was consequential because it marked rare earths as a practical instrument of great-power bargaining. After a year, its main legacy was not stable trade peace but a clearer understanding that critical-minerals controls would remain part of the technology and defense-policy contest.
