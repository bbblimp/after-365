## 1. Suriname votes in a close parliamentary election before an expected oil boom

**Original date:** 2025-05-25
**Type:** Elections / Resource Governance / Coalition Politics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-25, Suriname held a National Assembly election with no single party expected to dominate. Contemporary reporting emphasized that the vote would determine the parliament responsible for choosing the next president, while the country was preparing for a projected offshore oil windfall.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the election's consequence is clearer. The close result enabled a coalition led by Jennifer Geerlings-Simons's National Democratic Party; parliament elected her Suriname's first female president in July 2025. The governing challenge remained economic rather than symbolic alone: the IMF's 2026 Article IV consultation still framed Suriname as a state preparing for a major oil boom while needing fiscal discipline and stronger institutions.

### Direct impacts
The vote ended Chan Santokhi's governing position and produced a coalition path for Geerlings-Simons, whose party had narrowly outpaced the incumbent party in seats. It also set the political terms for decisions about public debt, future oil revenues, and whether oil income would be treated as a development resource or a short-term budget cushion.

### Indirect impacts
The election shifted Suriname's oil-boom debate from campaign rhetoric to institutional design. A small parliament and coalition government now have to manage expectations before first production, while international lenders continue to press for fiscal buffers and reform credibility.

### What original coverage missed
Election-day coverage could see the oil stakes and likely coalition bargaining, but it could not yet know that the deadlock would produce Suriname's first woman president. It also could not judge whether the new government would quickly solve the harder problem: preparing institutions before the money arrives.

### One-year assessment
The May 25 election was consequential because it connected democratic turnover, gender representation, and resource governance in a small state on the edge of a large economic transition. One year later, its importance rests less in the vote count itself than in the governing test it created.

## 2. Rival Warsaw marches frame Poland's presidential runoff as a reform-veto contest

**Original date:** 2025-05-25
**Type:** Elections / Rule of Law / European Politics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-25, tens of thousands of Poles joined rival marches in Warsaw for Rafal Trzaskowski and Karol Nawrocki ahead of the June 1 presidential runoff. Coverage described the vote as consequential because Poland's president could veto legislation central to Prime Minister Donald Tusk's judicial and social-policy agenda.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the marches read as a visible preview of a narrow but consequential result. Nawrocki won the runoff on June 1, 2025, and his victory preserved a presidency aligned against much of Tusk's reform program. Subsequent analysis and reporting treated the result as a renewed source of political gridlock rather than a simple personnel change.

### Direct impacts
The May 25 rallies mobilized the two electoral blocs before a runoff decided by a small margin. They showed that the presidency was not only ceremonial: the office's veto power made the election a practical test of whether the government could reverse Law and Justice-era judicial changes and pass parts of its liberal platform.

### Indirect impacts
The runoff result complicated Warsaw's effort to present a unified pro-EU reform trajectory. It also gave Poland's nationalist opposition a nationally elected office from which to contest Tusk's government before the next parliamentary election.

### What original coverage missed
The original coverage correctly identified the stakes, but it could not yet know that the nationalist-backed candidate would win. One year later, the marches are useful because they captured the unresolved polarization that became an institutional veto problem.

### One-year assessment
The May 25 marches were consequential as a public rehearsal for Poland's post-2023 governing constraint. They did not decide the election, but they made visible the choice that soon shaped Poland's reform capacity for the rest of the presidential term.

## 3. South Korea's overseas presidential voting closes at record turnout

**Original date:** 2025-05-25
**Type:** Elections / Democratic Recovery / Diaspora Voting
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-25, South Korea's overseas voting period closed for the June 3 snap presidential election. Yonhap later reported preliminary overseas turnout at 79.5 percent, a record for overseas voting, with ballots cast at 223 polling stations in 118 countries after the election was triggered by Yoon Suk Yeol's removal over his martial-law declaration.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the overseas turnout stands as an early signal of the election's unusually high civic intensity. Lee Jae-myung won the presidency on June 3, 2025, in what Reuters described as a rebuke to the martial-law crisis, and overall turnout reached its highest level in decades. The new administration then had to combine democratic-restoration symbolism with practical economic and security policy.

### Direct impacts
The overseas vote added diaspora participation to an election already framed by impeachment, constitutional order, and public rejection of martial law. It also provided an early data point that turnout would be high enough to strengthen the winning mandate.

### Indirect impacts
The record overseas participation showed that the crisis had widened political engagement beyond the peninsula. It also reinforced the legitimacy of a rapid electoral transition in a system that had just tested impeachment, military restraint, and civilian voting procedures.

### What original coverage missed
The May 25 voting close could not itself show who would win or whether the election would calm South Korea's political divisions. Later results showed a clear transfer of power, but not a simple end to the policy challenges created by polarization, prosecution disputes, tariffs, and regional security pressures.

### One-year assessment
The May 25 overseas-voting milestone was consequential because it was a measurable sign that South Korea's democratic recovery after martial law would be expressed through broad participation, not just elite legal proceedings. Its importance is procedural, but the procedure mattered.

## 4. Bangladesh civil-service and tax-authority protests widen under the interim government

**Original date:** 2025-05-25
**Type:** Governance / Public Administration / Reform Politics
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-25, Bangladesh saw overlapping protests by government employees and tax officials. AFP reported that security forces surrounded the national tax authority headquarters as National Board of Revenue employees extended a strike over interim-government tax reforms, while separate civil-servant protests challenged a public-service ordinance that expanded disciplinary powers.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the protests look like an early warning about reform capacity under the post-Hasina interim order. Human Rights Watch later said the interim government struggled to maintain law and order and deliver promised reforms, while 2026 reporting on revenue reform showed the NBR restructuring still under review and tied to IMF-supported reform expectations. The public-service and revenue disputes did not disappear as short labor stoppages.

### Direct impacts
The May 25 protests disrupted revenue and administrative work and forced the government into negotiations over the design and implementation of reform ordinances. They also showed that civil servants and revenue officials could become organized veto players in an unelected reform period.

### Indirect impacts
The episode complicated the interim government's claim that technocratic reforms could be imposed quickly after the fall of the Awami League government. It linked administrative modernization to legitimacy, labor rights, fiscal performance, and the timetable for restoring elected government.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate reports captured the disruption and employee grievances, but they could not yet show whether the reforms would survive normal politics. One year later, the continued review of revenue restructuring suggests the May protests were part of a longer institutional bargaining process.

### One-year assessment
The May 25 protests were consequential because they exposed a central tension in Bangladesh's transition: reform was demanded after authoritarian rule, but the machinery expected to implement reform resisted being reorganized by ordinance. The event's legacy is not a single policy reversal; it is a clearer view of how difficult administrative change became.
