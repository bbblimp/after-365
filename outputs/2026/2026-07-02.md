## 1. Iran suspends cooperation with the IAEA after the June strikes

**Original date:** 2025-07-02
**Type:** Nuclear Diplomacy / Nonproliferation / Middle East
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian ordered the suspension of cooperation with the International Atomic Energy Agency after U.S. and Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities. The move followed approval by Iran's parliament and constitutional bodies and was framed by Tehran as a response to attacks on nuclear sites and scientists rather than a formal withdrawal from the Non-Proliferation Treaty.

### State after 365 days
A year later, the suspension stands as one of the most durable institutional consequences of the June 2025 conflict. Inspectors' access became a bargaining point in renewed diplomacy, and by June 2026 reporting described an agreement in principle for inspectors to return as part of a wider U.S.-Iran arrangement tied to sanctions relief and regional de-escalation. The practical level of access still depended on implementation and Iranian security approval.

### Direct impacts
The immediate effect was to weaken routine international visibility into Iran's nuclear program at the moment when outside governments were disputing how much damage the strikes had done. It also gave Tehran a formal lever over IAEA access without taking the more escalatory step of leaving the NPT.

### Indirect impacts
The suspension made verification itself part of the diplomatic contest. It strengthened arguments by the United States, Israel, and European governments that Iran's program had become harder to monitor, while allowing Iran to argue that inspections could not continue normally after military attacks on safeguarded facilities.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports captured the immediate escalation but could not yet show whether the suspension would be symbolic or operational. The one-year record suggests it became operational enough to be traded in later negotiations and to keep uncertainty over stockpiles, damage, and inspection access at the center of the nuclear file.

### One-year assessment
The July 2 suspension was consequential because it converted a military confrontation into a verification crisis. It did not end diplomacy, but it changed the price and mechanics of restoring international monitoring.

## 2. The European Commission proposes a 90 percent 2040 climate target

**Original date:** 2025-07-02
**Type:** Climate Policy / European Union / Industrial Transition
**Confidence:** High

### Then
The European Commission proposed writing a 90 percent net greenhouse-gas reduction target for 2040 into EU climate law, measured against 1990 levels. The proposal kept the headline number urged by climate advisers but added flexibility, including limited use of international carbon credits from 2036 and a larger role for carbon removals.

### State after 365 days
One year later, the proposal had become the negotiating base for the EU's next major climate-law milestone. Member states reached a political agreement in November 2025 after adding more flexibility for reluctant governments, and a provisional Parliament-Council deal followed in December. The core 90 percent target survived, but the route to it became more dependent on offsets, removals, and political compromise.

### Direct impacts
The proposal set the formal 2040 benchmark between the EU's 2030 target and its 2050 climate-neutrality goal. It also defined the bloc's negotiating position for its 2035 contribution ahead of COP30 and forced governments and industries to take positions on the scale and timing of decarbonization.

### Indirect impacts
The flexibility provisions showed how the EU's Green Deal politics had shifted from expansion to defense and implementation. Climate ambition remained in the legal text, but competitiveness, energy prices, industrial policy, and right-leaning political pressure shaped the final design.

### What original coverage missed
Early coverage rightly focused on whether the 90 percent figure was ambitious enough or weakened by credits. The later record clarifies that the central question was political durability: whether a binding target could survive a more skeptical Council and Parliament.

### One-year assessment
The July 2 proposal mattered because it kept the EU's 2050 pathway legally structured while exposing the compromises needed to maintain that pathway. Its significance lies less in a single emissions cut than in locking the next decade of EU climate and industrial policy into a measurable benchmark.

## 3. Wisconsin's Supreme Court strikes down the state's 1849 abortion ban

**Original date:** 2025-07-02
**Type:** Reproductive Rights / State Courts / U.S. Law
**Confidence:** High

### Then
In a 4-3 decision, the Wisconsin Supreme Court held that the state's 1849 near-total abortion ban had been superseded by later abortion statutes. The ruling preserved abortion access under the state's post-1980s regulatory framework and closed a central legal uncertainty that had followed the U.S. Supreme Court's Dobbs decision.

### State after 365 days
A year later, the ruling remains a key example of state courts determining abortion access after Dobbs. It also fed into Wisconsin's continuing judicial politics: in April 2026, voters elected Chris Taylor to replace conservative Justice Rebecca Bradley, expanding the court's liberal majority to 5-2 before Taylor's scheduled August 2026 term.

### Direct impacts
The decision gave providers and patients clearer legal ground for abortion services in Wisconsin and rejected the claim that a dormant 19th-century criminal statute controlled modern practice. It also ended the main state-law case brought by Attorney General Josh Kaul over the old ban.

### Indirect impacts
The case reinforced the importance of state supreme court elections in post-Dobbs abortion policy. Wisconsin's high court had already become a nationalized judicial battleground, and the ruling showed why control of state courts can determine practical access even without new federal legislation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage captured the legal win for abortion-rights advocates. The one-year record adds that the ruling was part of a broader judicial realignment in Wisconsin, with abortion, voting rules, and redistricting all tied to the court's ideological balance.

### One-year assessment
The ruling was consequential because it settled a concrete access question in a swing state and turned an old criminal statute into a historical artifact rather than an enforceable statewide ban. It did not remove abortion from politics, but it moved the fight from statutory ambiguity to elections and future legislation.

## 4. Del Monte Foods files for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection

**Original date:** 2025-07-02
**Type:** Food Industry / Bankruptcy / Agricultural Supply Chains
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
Del Monte Foods, the long-running U.S. canned-fruit and vegetable business, entered Chapter 11 with a plan to sell most or all of its assets while continuing operations through debtor-in-possession financing. The filing reflected debt pressure, pandemic-era inventory problems, higher costs, and changing consumer demand.

### State after 365 days
By mid-2026, the bankruptcy had split the business among buyers and left visible supply-chain damage. Fresh Del Monte agreed to buy major vegetable, tomato, and refrigerated-fruit assets; other units went to Pacific Coast Producers and B&G Foods; and the failure to preserve some California processing capacity helped force Central Valley growers toward removing hundreds of thousands of clingstone peach trees.

### Direct impacts
The filing moved Del Monte into a court-supervised sale process and put creditors, workers, growers, and customers under restructuring uncertainty. It also marked the loss of independent continuity for one of the better-known U.S. shelf-stable food brands.

### Indirect impacts
The follow-on effects were clearest in agriculture. Processing contracts and cannery capacity matter for crops bred and planted for industrial buyers; when that capacity disappears, growers may have little realistic outlet even when the trees are productive.

### What original coverage missed
Early coverage could say that products might remain on shelves and that a sale was expected. What became clearer later was the regional supply-chain cost: asset sales could preserve brands while still closing facilities and stranding specialized growers.

### One-year assessment
The bankruptcy mattered less as a disappearance of a supermarket label than as a restructuring of processing capacity. Its one-year lesson is that consumer brands can survive bankruptcy while the agricultural systems behind them absorb much sharper losses.
