## 1. A former student kills 10 people at a school in Graz, Austria

**Original date:** 2025-06-10
**Type:** Public Safety / Gun Policy / School Violence
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-10, a 21-year-old former student opened fire at BORG Dreierschuetzengasse in Graz, killing 10 people before dying by suicide. Austrian leaders described the attack as a national tragedy, and early reporting noted that the attacker had legally obtained firearms shortly before the shooting.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the event remained more than an isolated act of violence. Austria had moved through national mourning, school closure and reconstruction planning, and a firearms-law response. Within a week of the shooting, Chancellor Christian Stocker said the government would toughen access to weapons through stricter eligibility rules, restrictions for risk groups, and better data-sharing among authorities.

### Direct impacts
The immediate impact was the death of students and a teacher, injuries to others, evacuation and emergency care for the school community, and three days of national mourning.

### Indirect impacts
The shooting forced Austria to revisit a firearms regime in which the attacker could acquire weapons despite later scrutiny of his mental-health and military-service history. It also produced school-security planning and continued trauma-management work for affected students, families, and staff.

### What original coverage missed
First-day coverage could establish the casualty toll, the legal possession of firearms, and the national shock. It could not yet show the later firearms-law debate, school reconstruction decisions, or the continuing legal and support questions around victims' families.

### One-year assessment
The Graz shooting was consequential because it converted a rare Austrian school massacre into a durable public-safety and gun-policy test. The policy response did not resolve every accountability question, but it shows the event retained institutional force well beyond the initial mourning period.

## 2. World Bank cuts 2025 growth forecasts for nearly 70 percent of economies

**Original date:** 2025-06-10
**Type:** Global Economy / Trade Policy / Development
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-10, the World Bank released its June 2025 Global Economic Prospects update, saying heightened trade tensions and policy uncertainty had led it to cut 2025 growth forecasts for nearly 70 percent of economies. It projected global growth of 2.3 percent in 2025, lower than expected at the start of the year.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June 2025 downgrade reads as an early marker of a slower and more fragmented trade-and-growth environment. The World Bank's later Global Economic Prospects material still described a resilient but vulnerable world economy, with developing economies lagging, trade risks unresolved, and job-creation pressures especially acute in emerging-market and low-income economies.

### Direct impacts
The report immediately reset a widely used baseline for governments, lenders, investors, and development institutions by lowering expected growth across regions and income groups.

### Indirect impacts
The downgrade reinforced the link between tariff escalation, policy uncertainty, debt burdens, and weaker poverty-reduction prospects. It also gave developing economies a stronger analytical case for trade diversification, fiscal resilience, and renewed multilateral financing.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could record the forecast cut, but it could not yet show whether the warning would become a durable framing for 2026 policy debates over trade barriers, public debt, and weak job creation.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 forecast was not a market shock by itself. Its importance was diagnostic: it captured how trade conflict and uncertainty were moving from political headlines into the medium-term development outlook.

## 3. The UK commits major public funding to Sizewell C nuclear power

**Original date:** 2025-06-10
**Type:** Energy Security / Climate Policy / Infrastructure
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-10, the UK government committed major public funding to the Sizewell C nuclear power station in Suffolk as part of a wider nuclear investment push. Coverage described a 14.2 billion pound commitment, expectations of power for roughly six million homes, and a promise of 10,000 jobs, while critics warned about cost, delivery time, and environmental tradeoffs.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the announcement had become part of a larger financing and delivery story. A final investment decision followed in July 2025, with the estimated project cost reported at about 38 billion pounds and a shareholder structure involving the UK government, La Caisse, Centrica, EDF, and Amber Infrastructure. The project remained a long-term bet on firm low-carbon power rather than a near-term energy-price fix.

### Direct impacts
The decision gave Sizewell C a clearer political and fiscal path after years of uncertainty and signaled that the government would carry a large share of early project risk.

### Indirect impacts
The commitment strengthened the UK's nuclear-industrial pipeline, supported small modular reactor ambitions, and reopened scrutiny of the regulated-asset-base funding model, in which consumers can bear costs before a plant generates electricity.

### What original coverage missed
Coverage on June 10 could identify the policy direction and funding headline, but it could not yet show the July final investment decision, revised full cost, investor split, or how much fiscal and consumer risk would remain.

### One-year assessment
The Sizewell C announcement mattered as a durable infrastructure commitment. Its success still depended on execution over the 2030s, but the June 2025 decision moved the project from recurring promise toward financed delivery.

## 4. Robert F. Kennedy Jr. removes the CDC vaccine advisory committee

**Original date:** 2025-06-10
**Type:** Public Health / Scientific Governance / Vaccine Policy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-10, U.S. Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s removal of all 17 members of the CDC's Advisory Committee on Immunization Practices was reported as a sweeping break with the normal expert-review process for vaccine recommendations. Kennedy said the step would restore trust; medical and public-health critics warned that it would politicize vaccine guidance.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the panel overhaul had become a court-tested governance dispute. Medical organizations sued, and in March 2026 a federal judge temporarily blocked parts of Kennedy's vaccine-policy overhaul, pausing actions by the reconstituted ACIP and finding that the appointment process likely violated federal law. Insurers and states were also forced to plan around uncertainty in federal vaccine guidance.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was the removal of the expert body whose recommendations shape CDC vaccine guidance, clinical practice, and insurance coverage for many vaccines.

### Indirect impacts
The purge widened distrust between federal health leadership and mainstream medical organizations. It also made vaccine coverage, school-entry guidance, and public communication more legally and administratively uncertain.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reporting could identify the institutional rupture and expert backlash, but it could not yet show the later lawsuit, injunction, or downstream confusion for 2026 flu and childhood vaccine planning.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 ACIP purge was consequential because it changed the process, not just a recommendation. One year later, the central question was whether U.S. vaccine policy would remain anchored in an independent expert committee or become more directly controlled by political appointees.
