## 1. Canada's One Canadian Economy Act passes second reading under an expedited timetable

**Original date:** 2025-06-16
**Type:** Infrastructure Policy / Internal Trade / Federalism
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-16, Canada's House of Commons gave Bill C-5, the One Canadian Economy Act, second reading and referred it to committee. The bill combined federal internal-trade and labour-mobility changes with a framework for accelerating projects deemed to be in the national interest. The same period also included Senate pre-study arrangements, showing that the government was using an unusually compressed legislative path.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the bill had become law and was no longer just an early-session marker for Prime Minister Mark Carney. It received royal assent on 2025-06-26 as Statutes of Canada 2025, chapter 2, and the government later launched the Major Projects Office to coordinate national-interest projects under the Building Canada Act. The framework remained politically consequential because it joined economic-security language, interprovincial trade reform, federal project approvals, and Indigenous consultation obligations in a single program.

### Direct impacts
The June 16 vote moved the bill from broad political promise into formal committee review. Within ten days the measure had royal assent. Its immediate legal effect was to create the Free Trade and Labour Mobility in Canada Act and the Building Canada Act, giving the federal government a mechanism to reduce federal internal-trade barriers and streamline selected major-project approvals.

### Indirect impacts
The law became part of Canada's response to trade pressure from the United States and a wider debate over whether Canada should prioritize national economic resilience, faster infrastructure approvals, and energy or transport projects. It also sharpened unresolved questions about parliamentary speed, provincial cooperation, environmental safeguards, and the substance of consultation with First Nations, Inuit, and Métis communities.

### What original coverage missed
Same-day coverage could see the fast-track vote and the government's economic framing, but it could not yet show how quickly the bill would become law or how the later Major Projects Office would turn the legislation into an administrative machinery for project selection and coordination.

### One-year assessment
The second-reading vote mattered because it was the procedural hinge for one of the Carney government's first durable policy instruments. One year later, its significance lies less in the vote count alone than in the new federal capacity it created for internal-market reform and national-interest project approvals, with continuing uncertainty over how that capacity will be used.

## 2. Senate Republicans release the tax-and-Medicaid core of the reconciliation bill

**Original date:** 2025-06-16
**Type:** Fiscal Policy / Health Policy / Legislative Process
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-16, Senate Finance Committee Republicans released their version of the central tax and health-policy text for the reconciliation bill commonly known as the One Big Beautiful Bill. The draft changed the House-passed package on child tax credit levels, business tax provisions, the state-and-local tax deduction cap, clean-energy credit phaseouts, and Medicaid financing.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June 16 text stood as an important intermediate version on the path to enacted law. H.R. 1 passed the Senate on 2025-07-01 by a 51-50 vote, the House accepted the Senate amendment on 2025-07-03 by 218-214, and the bill became Public Law 119-21 on 2025-07-04. Congress.gov describes the final act as reducing taxes, changing spending for federal programs, raising the statutory debt limit, and addressing agencies across the federal government.

### Direct impacts
The release gave senators, House members, lobbyists, state officials, and outside analysts concrete text to evaluate. It set up the late-June negotiations, parliamentarian rulings, amendment fights, and narrow votes that determined what could survive under reconciliation rules.

### Indirect impacts
The episode showed how much major U.S. fiscal policy was being made through reconciliation procedure rather than regular-order bipartisan legislation. It also made Medicaid provider taxes, energy credits, SALT, carried interest, deficit effects, and the debt limit part of one compressed legislative bargain.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage focused on what Senate Republicans changed from the House version and where votes might be difficult. The later record showed that the June 16 text was not the final bill, but it established the Senate negotiating baseline from which the enacted law emerged.

### One-year assessment
The text release was consequential because it translated a broad political agenda into Senate legislative language and exposed the tradeoffs that would shape final passage. One year later, its importance is procedural as well as substantive: it illustrates how a narrow Senate majority used reconciliation to enact a large tax-and-spending law.

## 3. The Paris Air Show opens with defense, space, and aviation-safety pressures in the foreground

**Original date:** 2025-06-16
**Type:** Aerospace / Defense Industry / Aviation Safety
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-16, the Paris Air Show opened at Le Bourget in a unusually tense setting. The show followed the Air India Flight 171 crash by four days, opened during direct Iran-Israel fighting and continuing wars in Gaza and Ukraine, and drew controversy after French authorities screened off Israeli offensive-weapons displays. Le Monde reported that defense, space, drones, cyber, AI, and quantum technologies were central themes at a show historically balanced between civil and military aviation.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the 2025 show reads less as a normal order-book event and more as a snapshot of a changing aerospace market. Follow-up reporting described an event dominated by defense and Airbus commercial orders, while Boeing kept a lower profile after the Air India crash. The show also highlighted Europe's interest in defense and space sovereignty, including drones and satellite infrastructure, under pressure from Ukraine, Gaza, the Middle East escalation, and uncertainty about U.S. security commitments.

### Direct impacts
The immediate effects were reputational and commercial rather than legislative: Boeing executives reduced visibility after the 787 crash, Airbus and other manufacturers announced aircraft deals, and defense exhibitors received unusual attention. The Israeli-booth dispute also made the arms-trade dimension of the air show more visible to a broader public.

### Indirect impacts
The opening reinforced a wider industrial shift from post-pandemic commercial recovery toward security, resilience, and sovereign capabilities. It also showed how civil aviation safety, defense exports, geopolitical alignment, and public protest can converge at a major aerospace marketplace.

### What original coverage missed
Opening-day coverage captured the atmosphere, but the full pattern became clearer only after the show: commercial orders were uneven, Boeing remained subdued, and defense and space themes carried much of the strategic significance.

### One-year assessment
The Paris Air Show was not a single policy decision, but it was an archival marker for aerospace priorities in 2025. One year later, its value is in showing how aviation safety, defense procurement, space sovereignty, and Middle East politics had become inseparable parts of the industry's public stage.
