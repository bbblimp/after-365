## 1. Los Angeles protests begin after federal immigration raids

**Original date:** 2025-06-06
**Type:** Immigration Enforcement / Protest / Federal Power
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-06, federal immigration operations in Los Angeles, including actions in the Fashion District, at a clothing wholesaler, and near a Home Depot, led to dozens of arrests and protests outside federal facilities. Police declared an unlawful assembly downtown after confrontations around the federal building and detention center.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the raids and first protests stand as the opening point of a broader confrontation over immigration enforcement and the use of federal force in domestic unrest. The Trump administration soon federalized California National Guard troops and sent Marines to Los Angeles; litigation followed over the legality of the deployment. By mid-July 2025, the Pentagon had begun ending part of the Guard deployment, while AP reported that demonstrations had mostly diminished into smaller protests around arrests.

### Direct impacts
The immediate result was arrests, street confrontations, local police dispersal orders, and a rapid escalation from immigration enforcement into public-order operations.

### Indirect impacts
The episode became a test case for presidential control of state National Guard forces during immigration protests, and it sharpened federal-state conflict between Washington and California over domestic military presence in a civilian city.

### What original coverage missed
Early coverage could describe the raids and confrontations, but it could not yet show that the protests would trigger Guard and Marine deployments, court challenges, curfews, and a national debate over the boundary between immigration enforcement and domestic military support.

### One-year assessment
The June 6 events were consequential less because of the first day of protest than because they created the factual basis for a much larger constitutional and political dispute. One year later, their significance lies in how routine enforcement actions escalated into a federal-force precedent.

## 2. Thailand and Cambodia escalate emergency border measures after a fatal clash

**Original date:** 2025-06-06
**Type:** Border Dispute / Armed Conflict / Regional Diplomacy
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-06, Thailand called an emergency National Security Council meeting and moved to tighten military control and border-crossing measures amid rising tension with Cambodia after a May 28 clash killed a Cambodian soldier. Cambodia rejected Thai demands to withdraw from disputed positions, while both governments framed the issue as a sovereignty matter.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June escalation looks like an early warning point in a border crisis that later became the deadliest Thailand-Cambodia fighting in more than a decade. July clashes killed dozens and displaced hundreds of thousands before a ceasefire; further violence in December led to a second ceasefire. By June 2026, Cambodia had moved a related maritime dispute into compulsory conciliation under the U.N. Convention on the Law of the Sea after Thailand revoked a long-running maritime memorandum.

### Direct impacts
The June measures hardened the border posture, narrowed room for routine local management, and increased the role of military and national-security institutions in a dispute that had previously been handled through bilateral mechanisms.

### Indirect impacts
The dispute fed nationalist politics in both countries and later pulled in ASEAN, China, the United States, and international legal forums as mediators or pressure points.

### What original coverage missed
Early June reporting captured the immediate border tension, but it could not yet show the later scale: heavy fighting, mass displacement, repeated ceasefires, and spillover into maritime-boundary diplomacy.

### One-year assessment
The June 6 emergency posture was consequential because it marked the shift from a localized fatal clash toward a wider interstate crisis. One year later, the dispute remained unresolved even where ceasefires held, and the legal-diplomatic track had expanded rather than replaced the security problem.

## 3. Togo security forces disperse rare protests against Faure Gnassingbe's continued rule

**Original date:** 2025-06-06
**Type:** Civil Liberties / Protest / Constitutional Change
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-05 and 2025-06-06, protesters in Lomé demonstrated against a crackdown on dissent, cost-of-living pressures, and constitutional changes that concentrated executive authority in the presidency of the Council of Ministers, a role assumed by Faure Gnassingbe on 2025-05-03. Security forces dispersed the rare protests and dozens of people were arrested.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June 6 protests are best understood as the start of a broader 2025 protest cycle and the symbolic anchor of the June 6 Movement. Amnesty International's 2025/26 review said at least 81 protesters were arrested on June 5-6, that fourteen detainees alleged torture or other ill-treatment supported by medical certificates, and that later June repression was followed by seven reported deaths whose investigation had not been publicly clarified by year's end.

### Direct impacts
The protests led to arrests, allegations of torture and ill-treatment, and further restrictions on peaceful assembly and expression.

### Indirect impacts
The crackdown helped transform a protest date into an opposition organizing symbol, while reinforcing the political meaning of the 2024 constitutional change: Gnassingbe's continued control moved from legal architecture into street-level contestation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could identify arrests and the triggering role of rapper Aamron's detention, but it could not yet show how the June 6 protests would be followed by wider repression, reported deaths later in June, and the continued restriction of civic organizing.

### One-year assessment
The June 6 protests were consequential because they exposed the social resistance beneath Togo's new constitutional order. One year later, the political system remained in place, but the crackdown left a documented human-rights record and a durable protest reference point.

## 4. Japan's ispace declares its second lunar landing attempt a failure

**Original date:** 2025-06-06
**Type:** Commercial Space / Lunar Exploration / Technology
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-06 Japan time, ispace's RESILIENCE lunar lander began its descent toward the Moon but lost communication before a confirmed touchdown. The company concluded the mission that morning, saying the lander likely made a hard landing after its laser rangefinder produced delayed valid readings and the vehicle failed to decelerate enough.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the failure stands as ispace's second unsuccessful lunar landing attempt, following its 2023 crash. The company later published a technical-cause analysis and continued to frame the data as input for future missions rather than a withdrawal from lunar delivery. In the broader commercial lunar sector, the result reinforced a mixed pattern: private Moon access was becoming more frequent, but soft landing remained technically unforgiving.

### Direct impacts
The mission did not deliver its rover or payloads to an operational state on the lunar surface, and ispace lost the opportunity to become the first non-U.S. commercial company to achieve a soft lunar landing.

### Indirect impacts
The failure increased scrutiny of sensor validation, descent autonomy, and risk management for private lunar landers, especially companies trying to sell payload delivery as a repeatable commercial service.

### What original coverage missed
Early reports could identify the likely hard landing, but they could not yet assess the specific technical-cause analysis or whether the company would continue its later mission plans.

### One-year assessment
The June 6 failure was consequential as an engineering and market signal rather than a geopolitical rupture. One year later, it shows that commercial lunar logistics is advancing, but still has a failure rate high enough to challenge customer confidence and schedule assumptions.
