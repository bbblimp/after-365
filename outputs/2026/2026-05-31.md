## 1. Georgia's new foreign-agent registration law takes effect

**Original date:** 2025-05-31
**Type:** Civil Society / Rule of Law / European Integration
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-31, Georgia's Foreign Agents Registration Act took effect, adding a second foreign-influence law to a political environment already strained by the 2024 foreign-influence statute and disputed elections. The new law required organizations and individuals meeting foreign-principal criteria to register, file information, and face penalties for noncompliance.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the law had become part of a broader civil-society and European-integration crisis rather than a narrow transparency measure. The Venice Commission warned in October 2025 that the act, alongside related amendments, risked a serious chilling effect on associations and media; the OSCE/ODIHR said in March 2026 that the broader legal framework restricted independent civil society; and Georgian authorities continued to face European criticism over democratic backsliding.

### Direct impacts
The law expanded registration, reporting, and enforcement risks for groups and individuals with foreign links. It also gave state institutions another legal tool for scrutinizing non-governmental organizations, media actors, and civic groups.

### Indirect impacts
The measure reinforced the perception that Georgia was moving away from the governance standards attached to EU candidacy. It increased legal uncertainty for donors and civil-society organizations and helped turn the country's European path into a question of institutional direction rather than only electoral politics.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could identify the law's text and critics' concerns, but it could not yet show how the act would sit within a cumulative legal package. The one-year record shows that its significance came from accumulation: registration rules, protest restrictions, grant-control measures, and pressure on civic space interacted.

### One-year assessment
The May 31 effective date was consequential because it converted a contested legislative agenda into enforceable administrative pressure. One year later, the law's clearest legacy is not a transparent registry, but its role in deepening the conflict between Georgia's government, domestic civil society, and European institutions.

## 2. Bulgarians protest the euro before accession is approved

**Original date:** 2025-05-31
**Type:** Monetary Integration / Protest Politics / European Union
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-31, thousands of Bulgarians joined nationalist-backed demonstrations against adopting the euro, arguing that the currency change would raise prices and weaken sovereignty. The protests came days before the European Commission and European Central Bank were expected to issue convergence assessments on whether Bulgaria was ready to join the euro area.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the protest did not stop the accession timetable. On 2025-06-04, the Commission and ECB concluded that Bulgaria met the convergence criteria; on 2025-07-08, the Council of the EU completed the legal steps for accession; and Bulgaria adopted the euro on 2026-01-01. By the run date, the question had shifted from whether Bulgaria would join to how households, businesses, and regulators were managing the conversion period.

### Direct impacts
The demonstrations showed that euro adoption was politically contested even after years of technical preparation. They put pressure on the government to address price, wage, and sovereignty concerns while EU institutions proceeded with the formal accession sequence.

### Indirect impacts
The episode illustrated a wider problem for monetary integration: meeting fiscal, inflation, exchange-rate, and legal criteria does not automatically settle public trust. Bulgaria's accession also changed the euro area's map, making it the twenty-first member and extending the currency union further into southeastern Europe.

### What original coverage missed
Same-day protest coverage could capture public resistance, but not the speed with which the formal EU process would move afterward. The one-year record shows that institutional approval and implementation outweighed the street-level challenge, though public confidence remained a policy issue during conversion.

### One-year assessment
The May 31 protests mattered as a final visible resistance point before accession became irreversible in practice. They did not alter the outcome, but they clarified that Bulgaria's euro adoption was both a technical convergence milestone and a domestic legitimacy test.

## 3. OPEC+ accelerates the return of voluntary oil supply

**Original date:** 2025-05-31
**Type:** Energy Markets / Oil Supply / Global Economy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-31, eight OPEC+ countries agreed to raise July 2025 production by 411,000 barrels per day, continuing an accelerated unwinding of voluntary output cuts. The decision extended a shift that had begun in April and May, when the group moved faster than the original schedule for returning withheld barrels.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the May decision looked like an early marker of a broader supply-policy reset. OPEC continued to frame the increases as gradual and conditional, while the International Energy Agency later described 2025 and 2026 as years of strong non-OPEC+ supply growth and potential surplus. The group's balancing problem had moved from simply defending prices to managing credibility, market share, and compliance among producers.

### Direct impacts
The July increase added planned supply to a market already watching demand uncertainty and non-OPEC+ growth. It also signaled that Saudi Arabia and allied producers were willing to tolerate lower price risk to unwind cuts and test member compliance.

### Indirect impacts
The move affected expectations for inflation, fuel costs, producer budgets, and energy-company planning. It also exposed tension inside OPEC+: countries with spare capacity and fiscal needs did not all share the same preference for restraint.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate market coverage could identify the barrel figure and price reaction, but it could not yet show that the decision was part of a longer transition away from emergency-era restraint. The one-year record shows that supply growth, not shortage management, became the central context.

### One-year assessment
The May 31 decision was consequential because it helped normalize a faster return of OPEC+ barrels and changed the market's interpretation of the group's strategy. It did not by itself determine oil prices, but it marked a durable shift in the supply narrative going into 2026.

## 4. The White House withdraws Jared Isaacman's NASA nomination

**Original date:** 2025-05-31
**Type:** Space Policy / Public Administration / Science Governance
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-31, the White House withdrew Jared Isaacman's nomination to be NASA administrator. The move came after the private astronaut and Shift4 founder had already gone through confirmation steps, and it left the agency without a Senate-confirmed leader while budget, Artemis, commercial-space, and science-priority decisions were active.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the withdrawal had become more than a personnel footnote. NASA operated through acting leadership in the months after the decision, and the administration later returned to Isaacman: the White House sent a new nomination to the Senate in November 2025, the Senate confirmed him in December 2025, and NASA said he was sworn in on 2025-12-18. The reversal underscored how closely NASA leadership had become tied to broader political fights over commercial space, procurement, and agency priorities.

### Direct impacts
The withdrawal delayed confirmation of a permanent administrator and extended uncertainty for NASA staff, contractors, and congressional overseers. It also forced interim leaders to manage policy and budget questions without the same institutional mandate as a confirmed appointee.

### Indirect impacts
The episode sharpened scrutiny of private-space ties and political loyalty tests in science agencies. It also complicated NASA's external signaling at a time when Artemis schedules, Mars planning, Earth-science funding, and commercial partnerships all required stable direction.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage could explain the surprise and political context, but not whether Isaacman's path was closed. The one-year record shows the opposite: the withdrawal was a costly delay rather than a final rejection.

### One-year assessment
The May 31 withdrawal was consequential because it introduced avoidable leadership instability at a high-capital, long-horizon agency. One year later, the same nominee's eventual confirmation made the episode look less like a change in space policy than a demonstration of how political volatility can disrupt agency continuity.
