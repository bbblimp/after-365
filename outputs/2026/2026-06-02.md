## 1. Mongolia's prime minister loses a confidence vote after corruption protests

**Original date:** 2025-06-02
**Type:** Parliamentary Politics / Anti-Corruption / Democratic Accountability
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-02, Mongolia's parliament took up Prime Minister Luvsannamsrai Oyun-Erdene's confidence vote after weeks of youth-led protests over corruption allegations and reports of lavish spending connected to his family. He received 44 votes, short of the 64 needed in the 126-seat State Great Khural, and was treated as having resigned.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the vote looks less like a single leadership change than an early marker of a more unsettled political year. Former parliament speaker Gombojavyn Zandanshatar was approved as prime minister on 2025-06-13 and promised to address protesters' economic concerns, but he resigned in March 2026 after ruling-party tensions and an opposition boycott. Mongolia therefore entered the anniversary with another prime ministerial transition pending or under way.

### Direct impacts
The confidence vote ended Oyun-Erdene's premiership, forced the ruling Mongolian People's Party to choose a replacement, and demonstrated that street protests could translate into parliamentary consequences in a young democracy.

### Indirect impacts
The episode sharpened investor and governance concerns in a mineral-dependent state between China and Russia. It also showed the fragility of Mongolia's enlarged parliament and party system after reforms intended to broaden representation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage captured the protest pressure and vote count, but could not yet show that replacing one prime minister would not settle the underlying instability. The later resignation of Zandanshatar suggested that the issue was not only one leader's vulnerability, but a wider ruling-party and parliamentary-management problem.

### One-year assessment
The June 2 vote was consequential because it converted anti-corruption protest into a constitutional accountability mechanism. One year later, the result is mixed: Mongolia's institutions removed a prime minister without extra-constitutional rupture, but the rapid churn that followed exposed continuing weakness in political consensus and executive durability.

## 2. The UK publishes a Strategic Defence Review built around warfighting readiness

**Original date:** 2025-06-02
**Type:** Defence Policy / NATO / Industrial Strategy
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-02, the UK government published the Strategic Defence Review 2025 and accepted its overall direction. The review framed defence around warfighting readiness, a NATO-first posture, lessons from Ukraine, a larger attack-submarine programme under AUKUS, investment in drones and munitions, and a GBP 15 billion sovereign warhead programme.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the review had become the reference document for UK defence reform, but implementation depended on money, industrial capacity, and a delayed Defence Investment Plan. Follow-up parliamentary and policy material showed concrete movement, including nuclear-deterrent decisions and new commands, while defence-industry reporting in 2026 pointed to frustration over the still-unsettled long-term funding path.

### Direct impacts
The review changed official planning language from efficiency and expeditionary operations toward readiness for state-on-state conflict. It also set procurement and industrial priorities for submarines, munitions, air and missile defence, cyber, intelligence, and nuclear-deterrent renewal.

### Indirect impacts
The review strengthened the link between UK defence policy, NATO burden-sharing, AUKUS, and domestic industrial policy. It also created a political test: whether the government could fund the force structure it had endorsed without stretching procurement, delaying programmes, or relying on future fiscal conditions.

### What original coverage missed
Launch-day coverage emphasized ambition and headline commitments. The one-year record shows that the harder question was sequencing: turning a broad strategic review into a costed investment plan, contracts, factories, trained personnel, and deployable capability.

### One-year assessment
The June 2 review was consequential because it reset the UK's defence baseline for a more dangerous European and North Atlantic environment. Its enduring value will depend less on the language of warfighting readiness than on whether the promised industrial and budget decisions arrive quickly enough to change actual force readiness.

## 3. Gabon moves to ban raw manganese exports from 2029

**Original date:** 2025-06-02
**Type:** Critical Minerals / Industrial Policy / Resource Nationalism
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-02, Eramet and market reporting responded to Gabon's plan to stop exports of unrefined manganese from 2029. The policy was presented as part of President Brice Oligui Nguema's effort to shift Gabon from raw-material exports toward domestic processing and industrial employment.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the announcement had become part of a broader critical-minerals pattern in which producer states seek more value from extraction. Gabonese officials met Comilog and Eramet to prepare implementation, and subsequent critical-minerals references treated Gabon's planned 2029 manganese-ore export ban as a live export-control measure rather than a passing political signal.

### Direct impacts
The announcement immediately affected Eramet, the main shareholder in Comilog, and raised questions about the future of the Moanda mine's export-oriented model, related rail and port logistics, and more than ten thousand jobs connected to Comilog and Setrag.

### Indirect impacts
The policy added Gabon to a list of African governments using export bans, processing requirements, or new mining codes to increase domestic capture of mineral value. It also mattered outside Gabon because manganese is central to steelmaking and has growing relevance in battery supply chains.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage naturally focused on Eramet's share price and immediate corporate exposure. The more durable issue is whether Gabon can build processing capacity, power supply, logistics, and technical skills before 2029 without disrupting export revenue.

### One-year assessment
The June 2 policy move was consequential because it put a deadline on a resource-processing ambition in one of the world's key manganese producers. Its success remains uncertain, but the announcement belongs to a wider shift in which mineral-rich states are no longer treating raw exports as the default endpoint of industrial policy.

## 4. The Boulder firebombing suspect is charged with a federal hate crime

**Original date:** 2025-06-02
**Type:** Hate Crime / Political Violence / Criminal Justice
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-02, federal prosecutors charged Mohamed Sabry Soliman with a hate crime after a June 1 firebombing attack on people gathered in Boulder, Colorado, to call attention to Israeli hostages in Gaza. Authorities said the attacker used Molotov cocktails and that investigators treated the case as targeted antisemitic violence.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the case had moved from attempted-murder and hate-crime allegations to a resolved state murder case and an unresolved federal prosecution. Karen Diamond, 82, died from injuries weeks after the attack; in May 2026 Soliman pleaded guilty to state charges and received life in prison without parole, while federal prosecutors were still weighing whether to seek the death penalty on hate-crime and explosives charges.

### Direct impacts
The attack killed one person, injured others, led to state murder and attempted-murder charges, and produced a federal hate-crime case. It also disrupted a public event tied to hostage advocacy and prompted immediate security and community responses.

### Indirect impacts
The case became part of a wider U.S. debate over antisemitic violence, political radicalization, immigration enforcement, and the boundary between protest-related grievance and targeted civilian attack.

### What original coverage missed
Coverage on June 2 could not yet know that one victim would die or that the state case would end in a guilty plea before the first anniversary. The later record also separated two tracks: a completed state punishment and a federal case still carrying possible capital consequences.

### One-year assessment
The June 2 federal charge was consequential because it defined the attack as a hate-crime matter from the start, not only as local assault. One year later, the central facts are clearer: the violence was lethal, the state criminal case produced a life sentence, and the federal case remained a test of how the United States prosecutes ideologically targeted public attacks.
