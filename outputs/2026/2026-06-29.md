## 1. Ukraine moves to withdraw from the Ottawa anti-personnel mine treaty

**Original date:** 2025-06-29
**Type:** Arms Control / Humanitarian Law / War Policy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
President Volodymyr Zelenskyy said he had signed a decree to begin pulling Ukraine out of the Ottawa Convention, the treaty banning anti-personnel mines. The decision was presented as a wartime response to Russia's use of mines and to similar moves by several European states bordering Russia.

### State after 365 days
One year later, the move stands as part of a broader weakening of the post-Cold War landmine norm in Europe. The treaty's status remains procedurally important: Ukraine's wartime withdrawal path was not the same as an immediate battlefield free hand, and the legal effect depends on treaty procedures as well as domestic implementation.

### Direct impacts
The decree shifted Ukraine's official position from treaty compliance toward seeking authority to use a class of weapons that the convention bans. It also put Ukrainian policy closer to that of Russia, the United States, and several nearby states that are outside or leaving the treaty framework.

### Indirect impacts
The decision sharpened the tradeoff between defensive military necessity and long-term civilian harm from mines. It also weakened the political isolation that anti-personnel mines had acquired under the Ottawa system, especially along Europe's eastern security frontier.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage emphasized the military rationale. The more durable issue was the institutional precedent: once active combat and regional fear made withdrawal politically acceptable, the treaty's normative force became harder to separate from the battlefield balance.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 decree was consequential because it made a humanitarian arms-control retreat explicit. It did not by itself resolve Ukraine's defensive problem or the treaty's legal complexities, but it marked a clear turn away from the assumption that anti-personnel mines would remain politically unusable in Europe.

## 2. Jeannette Jara wins Chile's governing-left presidential primary

**Original date:** 2025-06-29
**Type:** Elections / Party Coalitions / Latin American Politics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
Jeannette Jara, a former labor minister from Chile's Communist Party, won the governing-left Unity for Chile primary with a clear majority. The result made her the coalition's presidential nominee and displaced more centrist expectations inside the governing camp.

### State after 365 days
The primary proved to be the opening move in a sharply polarized national contest. Jara led the first round in November 2025, but right-wing candidates collectively showed greater breadth; in the December runoff, Jose Antonio Kast defeated her decisively and took office in March 2026.

### Direct impacts
The result unified the governing coalition around Jara and gave labor policy, wages, pensions, public services, crime, and migration a central place in the campaign debate. It also confirmed that Chile's left would contest the election through a candidate from its more leftward wing.

### Indirect impacts
Jara's nomination helped clarify the contrast Kast wanted: a hard-line public-order and migration platform against a governing-left continuity candidate. That contrast shaped coalition behavior on both sides and helped nationalize questions about crime and economic frustration.

### What original coverage missed
First-day coverage captured the surprise and Jara's internal victory, but it could not yet show that the primary's main legacy would be a runoff map favorable to Kast rather than a left consolidation around Boric-era reforms.

### One-year assessment
The primary was consequential even though Jara lost the presidency. It selected one pole of Chile's 2025 election and made visible the governing coalition's difficulty in converting a disciplined primary win into a national majority under compulsory voting and rising security concerns.

## 3. Thom Tillis says he will not seek reelection after breaking with Trump on a major bill

**Original date:** 2025-06-29
**Type:** U.S. Politics / Senate Control / Intraparty Discipline
**Confidence:** High

### Then
Republican Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina announced that he would not seek reelection in 2026 after opposing President Donald Trump's tax and spending bill and drawing a public threat of a primary challenge. His retirement turned a difficult incumbent race into an open-seat contest.

### State after 365 days
One year later, the seat is one of the central contests of the 2026 Senate cycle. Former North Carolina governor Roy Cooper entered and became the Democratic nominee, while Trump-backed former Republican National Committee chair Michael Whatley became the Republican nominee.

### Direct impacts
Tillis's decision removed a sitting Republican senator with an independent streak from the ballot and immediately changed candidate recruitment, fundraising, and national-party strategy in North Carolina.

### Indirect impacts
The episode became an example of the limited space for Republican dissent from Trump on major legislation. It also gave Democrats a stronger opening in a state where they have often come close in federal races without winning Senate seats.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage correctly emphasized Trump's pressure and Medicaid concerns in the bill. The longer-term significance was the way a single intraparty conflict reshaped an entire battleground Senate map within weeks.

### One-year assessment
Tillis's announcement was not merely a retirement notice. It converted North Carolina into a test of candidate quality, presidential coattails, health-care politics, and party discipline in the 2026 midterm environment.

## 4. Wildfires in Turkey's Izmir province force mass evacuations

**Original date:** 2025-06-29
**Type:** Wildfires / Climate Risk / Emergency Management
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
Wildfires broke out in western Turkey, especially in Izmir province, during hot, dry, windy conditions. Authorities evacuated tens of thousands of people from affected settlements as smoke and fire disrupted roads, villages, and airport operations.

### State after 365 days
The Izmir fires became an early part of a wider Turkish and Mediterranean fire season. Within weeks, Turkey had reported additional fatalities, damaged homes and barns, disaster-zone declarations in Izmir and Bilecik, and further evacuations as heat and wind continued to complicate firefighting.

### Direct impacts
The fires forced large evacuations, damaged rural property, interrupted transport, and stretched firefighting capacity. Later reporting linked some fire starts to electrical lines igniting dry grass, while authorities also investigated human causes in several incidents.

### Indirect impacts
The episode fed into a larger debate over fire preparedness, land management, infrastructure maintenance, and climate adaptation in Mediterranean regions where heat, low humidity, and wind can turn small ignitions into mass-evacuation events.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports focused on the emergency response and evacuation count. The following weeks showed that the issue was not a single wildfire perimeter but the start of a sustained seasonal stress test for Turkey's disaster management system.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 Izmir fires matter as an adaptation signal. Their lasting significance lies in how quickly ordinary ignition risks became large public emergencies under severe weather conditions, and in how similar pressures recurred through the rest of the summer.
