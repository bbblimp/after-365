## 1. The UK and Mauritius sign the Chagos Archipelago treaty

**Original date:** 2025-05-22
**Type:** Decolonization / Strategic Bases / Treaty Politics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-22, the United Kingdom and Mauritius signed a treaty concerning the Chagos Archipelago, including Diego Garcia. The UK government said the agreement would recognize Mauritian sovereignty while securing continued UK-U.S. operation of the Diego Garcia military base for at least 99 years, at an average annual cost of 101 million pounds.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the treaty has not settled the dispute in practice. UK parliamentary material shows that implementation legislation was introduced after the treaty, but The Guardian reported in April 2026 that a UK minister said the treaty had become politically impossible to agree and that the related bill would not complete its passage, after U.S. support for the arrangement became uncertain.

### Direct impacts
The treaty created a formal legal framework for ending UK sovereignty claims over the wider archipelago while preserving operational control over Diego Garcia. It also put a large lease payment, base-protection rules, and implementation legislation into the center of UK domestic politics.

### Indirect impacts
The agreement linked decolonization, Chagossian displacement, Indian Ocean strategy, and U.S.-UK defense planning in a single legal instrument. Its later stall showed that a treaty designed to reduce legal risk can still fail when domestic opposition, Chagossian objections, and allied military concerns are not politically aligned.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage could describe the signing, the last-minute legal challenge, and the strategic rationale, but it could not know whether the treaty would survive ratification politics. By the anniversary, the important fact was not only that the treaty had been signed, but that its implementation had become uncertain.

### One-year assessment
The May 22 treaty was consequential because it tried to convert a long-running decolonization dispute into a base-lease settlement. One year later, its significance lies in the unresolved collision between international legal pressure, the security value of Diego Garcia, and the political legitimacy of negotiating sovereignty over islands whose displaced people remain divided or excluded.

## 2. Brazil's Senate advances a broad environmental-licensing rollback

**Original date:** 2025-05-22
**Type:** Environment / Legislation / Development Policy
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-22, environmental groups criticized Brazil's Senate approval of a broad environmental-licensing bill, widely described by opponents as the 'devastation bill.' Reporting at the time said the proposal would simplify or remove licensing requirements for many projects, raise risks for protected areas and traditional communities, and test President Luiz Inacio Lula da Silva's climate-policy credibility.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the bill has moved from warning to governing law. Lula sanctioned the measure in August 2025 with 63 vetoes, but Brazil's Congress overturned 52 vetoes in November 2025. Agencia Brasil reported in February 2026 that the General Environmental Licensing Law had entered into force while three direct actions of unconstitutionality were pending before the Supreme Federal Court.

### Direct impacts
The legislative process changed Brazil's licensing framework by expanding simplified routes, returning vetoed provisions to the law, and reopening rules on consultation, state and local discretion, and categories of projects that may face less detailed review.

### Indirect impacts
The fight weakened the image of a unified Brazilian climate agenda before and after COP30 in Belem. It also gave environmental groups, Indigenous and quilombola advocates, developers, state governments, and courts a new legal battlefield over what counts as efficient permitting versus environmental deregulation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could identify the Senate vote and the warnings from environmental advocates, but it could not know which vetoes would survive. By the anniversary, the key development was that congressional override power had restored much of the contested framework and moved the dispute to the Supreme Court.

### One-year assessment
The May 22 Senate vote was consequential because it marked a decisive stage in Brazil's retreat from older licensing safeguards. One year later, the measure has become a live statute and constitutional dispute, with practical effects likely to emerge through project approvals, litigation, and regulatory fragmentation rather than through a single dramatic event.

## 3. North Korea's second new destroyer is damaged during a launch ceremony

**Original date:** 2025-05-22
**Type:** Defense / Naval Modernization / State Capacity
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-22, North Korean state media acknowledged that a newly built 5,000-ton-class destroyer had been damaged during a launch ceremony attended by Kim Jong Un at Chongjin. AP reported that Kim called the accident a serious failure and demanded accountability, an unusual public admission for a state that normally hides military setbacks.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the failed launch looks less like the end of the program than a revealing interruption. USNI News reported in June 2025 that North Korea had refloated and relaunched the ship, named Kang Kon, at Rajin after the May failure. Outside analysts remained skeptical of the repair quality and timing, but the episode confirmed that larger surface combatants had become an explicit Kim-era naval priority.

### Direct impacts
The accident damaged a new destroyer, embarrassed officials responsible for the launch, and forced urgent salvage and repair work. It also produced rare state-media acknowledgement of a military-industrial failure.

### Indirect impacts
The episode sharpened outside scrutiny of North Korea's shipbuilding capacity, quality control, and propaganda discipline. Its rapid relaunch served a political purpose: showing that the naval modernization campaign would continue despite visible technical failure.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports could document the damaged hull and Kim's anger, but they could not know whether the ship would be abandoned, quietly hidden, or returned to service. The later relaunch showed that the regime treated the failure as a discipline and propaganda problem as much as an engineering one.

### One-year assessment
The launch accident was consequential less because one vessel was damaged than because it exposed the pressures inside North Korea's push for a more capable navy. One year later, the program appears to have continued, but the accident remains evidence that political timelines and technical readiness were not fully aligned.

## 4. The U.S. Senate votes to block California vehicle-emissions waivers

**Original date:** 2025-05-22
**Type:** Climate Policy / Federalism / Congressional Procedure
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-22, the U.S. Senate voted to block California vehicle-emissions waivers, including the rule phasing out sales of new gas-powered cars by 2035. AP reported that the votes used the Congressional Review Act despite objections that EPA waivers were not rules subject to that mechanism, and California officials said they would sue.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the vote has become both policy and litigation. President Donald Trump signed three joint resolutions on 2025-06-12, and the White House said the CRA barred EPA from approving substantially similar future waivers. California and allied states filed suit the same day, arguing that Congress and the president had unlawfully nullified Clean Air Act waivers; state litigation trackers still identified the case as an active challenge in 2026.

### Direct impacts
The Senate vote sent the resolutions to the president and set up the formal disapproval of California's Advanced Clean Cars II waiver and related truck-emissions waivers. It immediately threatened California's 2035 zero-emission vehicle pathway and parallel standards adopted by other states.

### Indirect impacts
The episode widened the fight from vehicle rules to congressional procedure, federalism, and the legal status of administrative waivers. If upheld, the precedent could make some agency permissions more vulnerable to fast-track congressional reversal; if rejected, it could reaffirm limits on using the CRA outside ordinary rulemaking.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage captured the political stakes and procedural objections, but it could not know how quickly the resolutions would become public law or how the litigation would be framed. By the anniversary, the dispute was no longer hypothetical: the signed resolutions and California's complaint defined the legal battlefield.

### One-year assessment
The May 22 vote was consequential because it joined climate policy to a Senate precedent over the Congressional Review Act. One year later, the practical fate of California's vehicle standards remains tied to court outcomes, but the vote already changed the risk calculus for state-led climate regulation.
