## 1. Vietnam scraps its two-child policy amid falling fertility

**Original date:** 2025-06-04
**Type:** Population Policy / Demography / Social Policy
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-04, Vietnamese state-linked and international reporting said the National Assembly Standing Committee had approved amendments to the Population Ordinance removing the rule that limited families to one or two children. The revision gave couples discretion over the number, timing, and spacing of children, and was framed as a response to fertility below replacement level.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the change looks less like an immediate birth-rate fix than a marker of Vietnam's demographic turn. Follow-up policy analysis described the amendment as groundwork for a broader Population Law focused on replacement fertility, ageing, population quality, and regional fertility gaps. The available record does not yet show a measurable reversal in births; the policy's significance is that Vietnam formally moved away from restriction as its working-age advantage narrowed.

### Direct impacts
The amendment removed a long-standing legal norm around one or two children and changed official population policy from family-size restriction toward reproductive discretion within a development-policy framework.

### Indirect impacts
The change added Vietnam to the group of Asian economies adjusting policy after fertility decline, ageing, and pressure on future labor supply became more urgent than earlier concerns about overpopulation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could identify the repeal and the 2024 fertility-rate concern, but it could not yet show whether social costs, housing, work conditions, and regional inequality would limit any fertility response.

### One-year assessment
The June 4 repeal was consequential because it formally ended a population-control instrument that no longer matched Vietnam's demographic problem. One year later, its importance is mainly institutional: it reset the legal baseline for later fertility and ageing policy, while evidence of demographic impact remains uncertain.

## 2. A crowd crush outside Bengaluru's Chinnaswamy Stadium kills 11 fans

**Original date:** 2025-06-04
**Type:** Public Safety / Crowd Management / Sport
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-04, at least 11 people were killed and more than 30 were injured outside M. Chinnaswamy Stadium in Bengaluru as tens of thousands gathered to celebrate Royal Challengers Bengaluru's first Indian Premier League title. Authorities said crowds tried to enter the stadium during the victory celebrations.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the stampede remains an unresolved accountability case as well as a venue-safety case. Indian Express follow-up reporting in March 2026 said criminal charges had still not been brought to court because Karnataka High Court orders blocked filing of chargesheets, while a judicial commission had recommended legal action against officials of RCB, DNA Networks, KSCA, and Bengaluru police. The stadium later received conditional permission to host matches subject to safety compliance.

### Direct impacts
The crush killed 11 mostly young fans, injured dozens, led to FIRs against event and stadium entities, and forced official inquiries into crowd control at major sporting events in Bengaluru.

### Indirect impacts
The case shifted attention from celebratory sports management to regulatory readiness: how quickly clubs, event firms, stadium bodies, police, and state officials can safely convert a mass online invitation into a controlled public gathering.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports captured the casualty count and crowd surge, but they could not yet show the slow legal path, the high-court procedural barriers, or the tension between restoring marquee cricket fixtures and resolving accountability.

### One-year assessment
The June 4 stampede was consequential because it exposed how thin crowd-safety planning can be around high-emotion mass sport events. One year later, the deaths have produced inquiries and conditional safety measures, but legal accountability remains incomplete.

## 3. Three former Salvadoran officers are convicted over the 1982 killings of Dutch journalists

**Original date:** 2025-06-04
**Type:** Transitional Justice / Press Freedom / Civil War Accountability
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-04, the Associated Press reported that a jury in Chalatenango had convicted former Salvadoran defense minister Jose Guillermo Garcia and two other former military officers for the 1982 killings of four Dutch journalists during El Salvador's civil war. The initial report said each received a 15-year sentence and that the judge ordered a state apology for delayed justice.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the convictions stand as one of the clearest late accountability outcomes from the Salvadoran civil war. A July 2025 AP follow-up reported that the written sentence treated the penalties cumulatively but applied the 30-year maximum allowed at the time of the killings; two convicted men were to serve under hospital guard, while Mario Adalberto Reyes Mena remained in the United States with an extradition process ordered.

### Direct impacts
The verdict imposed criminal responsibility on senior former officers more than four decades after the journalists were ambushed and killed, and it ordered the commander in chief to issue a public apology to the families.

### Indirect impacts
The case reinforced the value of reopening civil-war crimes after amnesty barriers fall. It also connected press-freedom memory, foreign victims' families, and domestic human-rights organizations in a system where many wartime crimes remain unresolved.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could report the convictions, but the written sentence later clarified the practical exposure to a 30-year maximum and showed how unresolved extradition would still limit full enforcement.

### One-year assessment
The June 4 convictions were consequential because they pierced decades of impunity in a documented civil-war killing of journalists. One year later, the case is a meaningful legal precedent, though incomplete enforcement and the age and health of the defendants narrow its practical reach.

## 4. Pornhub's owner blocks access in France over age-verification rules

**Original date:** 2025-06-04
**Type:** Digital Regulation / Child Protection / Privacy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-04, Aylo, the owner of Pornhub, YouPorn, and Redtube, suspended access to its user-uploaded adult platforms in France in protest against French age-verification requirements. The company argued that the French approach created privacy and security risks, while regulators framed the rules as a child-protection measure.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the immediate shutdown proved temporary but the policy problem persisted. The sites returned later in June 2025 after the Paris administrative tribunal suspended the decree while reviewing its compatibility with EU law. The episode remains significant because it showed that age assurance for large adult platforms can trigger platform withdrawal, court review, and unresolved tradeoffs between child protection, privacy, enforcement, and market incentives.

### Direct impacts
French users lost access to several major adult platforms for roughly two weeks, the company used its sites as a public protest channel, and the age-verification decree was quickly tested in court.

### Indirect impacts
The dispute became a practical example for other governments considering online age checks: regulated platforms may comply, geoblock, litigate, or push users toward less regulated alternatives.

### What original coverage missed
Coverage at the moment of the blockade could not yet show how quickly a court would interrupt the enforcement path or how fragile national age-verification regimes can be when platforms are based elsewhere in the EU.

### One-year assessment
The June 4 blockade was consequential not because the French shutdown lasted, but because it exposed a recurring governance problem for online safety law. One year later, the episode reads as an early stress test of how age-verification mandates collide with privacy claims, jurisdiction, and platform leverage.
