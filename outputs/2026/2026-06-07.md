### One-year assessment

## 2. Iran says it obtained sensitive Israeli nuclear documents

**Original date:** 2025-06-07
**Type:** Intelligence / Nuclear Security / Regional Conflict
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-07, Iranian state media said Iranian intelligence agencies had obtained a large trove of sensitive Israeli documents, including material related to nuclear plans and facilities. Reuters reported the claim with caution, noting there was no immediate Israeli comment and that the link to a reported earlier hack was unclear.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the claim remains partly unverifiable in public, but it sits inside a much larger nuclear-security rupture. On 2025-06-09, the IAEA chief said the information Iran claimed to have obtained appeared to refer to Israel's Soreq Nuclear Research Center. Four days later, Israel launched major strikes on Iran's nuclear and military infrastructure, and by June 2026 the IAEA reported that it had still been unable to inspect Iranian nuclear facilities affected by the June 2025 war.

### Direct impacts
The immediate effect was informational and psychological: Iran publicly asserted an intelligence success against Israel's most sensitive strategic domain while nuclear diplomacy was already under strain.

### Indirect impacts
The episode added to the atmosphere of escalation before the Israel-Iran war and highlighted how intelligence claims, cyber operations, nuclear ambiguity, and public messaging can become part of crisis bargaining.

### What original coverage missed
The first reports could not verify the documents or anticipate how quickly the regional situation would move from intelligence claims and nuclear pressure into direct military strikes.

### One-year assessment
The June 7 claim should be treated carefully because much of it remains unproven publicly. Its significance lies less in the documents themselves than in its timing: it was one visible warning sign in a crisis that, within days, became a direct Israel-Iran war with continuing consequences for nuclear inspection and arms-control confidence.

## 3. China says it may speed rare earth export approvals for EU firms

**Original date:** 2025-06-07
**Type:** Critical Minerals / Trade Policy / Industrial Supply Chains
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-07, China's commerce ministry said it was willing to accelerate examination and approval of rare earth export applications for European Union firms. It also said it would issue a final decision by July 5 in an EU brandy trade investigation, placing critical minerals and tariff disputes in the same negotiation space.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the June statement looks less like a return to normal trade than an example of managed access. Reuters reported in May 2026 that China's rare earth export controls remained lawful in Beijing's view, that it would address reasonable U.S. concerns, and that Washington had obtained only a limited opening while the licensing regime continued to disrupt aerospace, semiconductor, and manufacturing supply chains.

### Direct impacts
The immediate impact was to offer relief to European manufacturers seeking rare earth materials and magnets, especially sectors such as electric vehicles and robotics.

### Indirect impacts
The episode showed that China could use export licensing not only as a security tool but as a bargaining instrument in disputes with major economies. It reinforced European and U.S. efforts to diversify critical-mineral supply chains, but those efforts remained slower than the licensing pressure.

### What original coverage missed
The original announcement could sound like de-escalation. The later pattern showed that faster approvals did not remove the underlying control architecture or the uncertainty it created for firms dependent on Chinese processing capacity.

### One-year assessment
The June 7 statement was consequential because it made the new rare-earth order visible in practice: access could be accelerated, delayed, or conditioned. One year later, that discretion remained one of China's most effective levers in high-technology trade disputes.

## 4. A senior Taiwanese official visits the proposed Alaska LNG project

**Original date:** 2025-06-07
**Type:** Energy Security / Infrastructure / Trade Diplomacy
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then

The visit followed a non-binding Taiwan CPC agreement to buy LNG from and invest in the project, which the Trump administration was promoting to Asian partners.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the visit reads as part of a wider commercial revival effort rather than a completed infrastructure decision. Glenfarne later announced preliminary commercial commitments with buyers in Japan, Korea, Taiwan, and Thailand, and in early 2026 said Phase One had moved toward early execution. Reuters reported in March 2026 that the project still needed additional offtake before a final investment decision on the full LNG project.

### Direct impacts
The immediate result was a diplomatic and commercial signal that Taiwan was willing to examine Alaska LNG as part of its energy-security and U.S. trade-balancing strategy.

### Indirect impacts
The visit helped frame Alaska LNG as a geopolitical project as much as an energy project: U.S. officials could pitch it as Asian supply diversification, while Asian buyers could use tentative participation to manage relations with Washington.

### What original coverage missed
The first report could not yet show whether expressions of interest would become binding contracts or whether the long-running cost and construction challenges would ease.

### One-year assessment
The June 7 visit mattered because it showed how tariff diplomacy, Taiwan's energy vulnerability, and U.S. Arctic infrastructure ambitions were being tied together. One year later, the project had more commercial momentum, but its final-investment risk remained unresolved.
