## 1. WHO members approve the pandemic agreement in committee

**Original date:** 2025-05-19
**Type:** Global Health / International Governance
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-19, World Health Organization member states meeting in Committee A of the World Health Assembly approved a resolution calling for adoption of the WHO Pandemic Agreement. Reuters reported 124 votes in favor, no votes against, and 11 abstentions, with final plenary adoption expected the next day.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the agreement is best understood as adopted but not operational. WHO reported on 2026-05-01 that member states needed more time to finish the Pathogen Access and Benefit Sharing annex, the mechanism needed before countries can proceed to signature and ratification. The United States also completed its withdrawal from WHO in January 2026, limiting the agreement's reach over the largest traditional WHO funder.

### Direct impacts
The vote moved the pandemic agreement from negotiation into formal World Health Assembly adoption. It preserved a multilateral framework after three years of talks and recorded broad support despite abstentions and the United States' nonparticipation in the final stage.

### Indirect impacts
The unresolved PABS annex kept the agreement from becoming a functioning legal regime. The one-year record therefore shows both the durability of post-COVID cooperation and the difficulty of settling questions about pathogen samples, sequence data, vaccines, diagnostics, and therapeutics.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could see the symbolism of the vote, but it could not know whether the annex would be ready for the 2026 World Health Assembly. By the anniversary, WHO had acknowledged that more negotiations were needed, with a seventh meeting scheduled for July 2026 and possible submission in 2027 or at a special 2026 session.

### One-year assessment
The May 19 vote was consequential because it kept a global pandemic-governance instrument alive when multilateral health cooperation was under strain. Its one-year limit is equally clear: without the PABS annex and national ratifications, the agreement remains a framework rather than an operating system for the next pandemic.

## 2. ICJ identifies Equatorial Guinea's legal title in the Gabon islands dispute

**Original date:** 2025-05-19
**Type:** International Law / Territorial Disputes
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-19, the International Court of Justice delivered judgment in the Gabon-Equatorial Guinea case concerning legal titles over land and maritime boundaries and the islands of Mbanie, Cocoteros, and Conga. AP reported that the decision effectively favored Equatorial Guinea on the islands by treating the 1900 Franco-Spanish treaty as the controlling legal title and rejecting Gabon's reliance on the contested 1974 Bata Convention.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the judgment had moved from courtroom result to implementation diplomacy. On 2026-03-25, the African Union appointed Albert Shingiro as special envoy to support Gabon and Equatorial Guinea in implementing the ICJ judgment, following a February 2026 quadripartite meeting where both states reaffirmed a commitment to coordinated and peaceful implementation.

### Direct impacts
The ruling clarified which legal title had force between the two states for the disputed islands and related boundary questions. It reduced legal ambiguity around a dispute that had been inflamed by hydrocarbon interests and a 1972 Gabonese occupation of Mbanie.

### Indirect impacts
The judgment did not by itself settle every practical modality on the ground or at sea. The need for an African Union envoy shows that implementation, boundary management, and political acceptance still required mediation after the formal legal decision.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports understandably framed the ruling as Equatorial Guinea winning the islands. The later follow-up adds a more restrained picture: the judgment was a legal settlement point, but peaceful implementation remained a diplomatic process requiring regional support.

### One-year assessment
The ICJ decision was consequential because it converted a decades-old bilateral territorial dispute into a rule-of-law implementation problem rather than a military or unilateral bargaining problem. One year later, the absence of a reported open crisis is meaningful, but the AU's involvement shows that legal clarity did not remove the need for managed diplomacy.

## 3. The UK and EU hold their first post-Brexit summit and announce a reset package

**Original date:** 2025-05-19
**Type:** Diplomacy / Trade / Security
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-19, UK and EU leaders met in London for the first EU-UK summit since Brexit. The two sides announced a new strategic partnership, a security and defence partnership, and a renewed cooperation agenda covering fisheries access, a possible SPS area, energy and electricity cooperation, ETS linkage, Erasmus+, youth mobility, internal security, and border issues.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the reset had produced a structured negotiating agenda and some practical movement, but not a complete new relationship. February 2026 Partnership Council minutes recorded progress toward Erasmus+ association from 2027, launched talks on ETS linkage and a common SPS area, continued work on electricity cooperation, and stability from the fisheries access deal. At the same time, reporting in late 2025 showed failure to agree on UK access to the EU SAFE defence-funding instrument, and later reporting described pressure to accelerate talks before the next summit.

### Direct impacts
The summit marked a formal change in tone after years of post-Brexit friction and gave both sides political cover to reopen cooperation areas without reopening EU membership. It also extended full reciprocal fishing access beyond June 2026 to June 2038, creating immediate predictability for fleets.

### Indirect impacts
The reset created expectations that trade frictions and defence-industrial cooperation could be improved through technical agreements. It also showed the limits of a selective reset: youth mobility, SPS alignment, defence funding, and regulatory questions all required further negotiation and domestic political consent.

### What original coverage missed
Original coverage could describe the package and its symbolism, but it could not know which parts would move from headline to legal text. One year later, the record is mixed: the institutional channels are active, yet the most politically sensitive files remain unfinished or contested.

### One-year assessment
The May 19 summit mattered because it changed the UK-EU relationship from post-Brexit damage management toward managed cooperation. Its one-year significance lies less in immediate economic change than in the creation of a durable agenda whose hardest items still require proof of delivery.

## 4. El Salvador arrests anti-corruption lawyer Ruth Lopez

**Original date:** 2025-05-19
**Type:** Civil Liberties / Rule of Law
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-19, Salvadoran authorities confirmed the arrest of Ruth Eleonora Lopez, head of the Anti-Corruption Unit at Cristosal, one of the country's leading human rights organizations. Prosecutors alleged corruption tied to her earlier government work, while Cristosal condemned the detention and said her family and lawyers initially did not know where she had been taken.

### State after 365 days
One year later, Human Rights Watch reported that Lopez remained in pretrial detention with her case under judicial seal. HRW said the initial embezzlement allegation was later changed to illicit enrichment, that a judge extended her pretrial detention until June 2026, and that her detention was followed by broader measures against critics, journalists, and civil-society organizations.

### Direct impacts
The arrest removed a prominent anti-corruption and human-rights lawyer from public work and placed her in a sealed criminal proceeding. It also sent an immediate signal to Cristosal and other critics of President Nayib Bukele's government that anti-corruption and due-process advocacy carried personal legal risk.

### Indirect impacts
The case became part of a wider contraction of civic space. Human Rights Watch reported that after the detention, El Salvador passed a foreign agents law, several civil-society organizations closed Salvadoran offices, and Cristosal suspended operations in El Salvador and relocated work to Guatemala and Honduras.

### What original coverage missed
Initial coverage could capture the arrest and the government's stated allegations, but it could not yet show the duration of detention, the sealed nature of proceedings, or the scale of subsequent pressure on rights groups and journalists.

### One-year assessment
Lopez's arrest was consequential because it became a concrete test of El Salvador's rule-of-law trajectory under the continuing state of emergency. After 365 days, the case looks less like an isolated corruption proceeding than a marker of institutional pressure on independent scrutiny.
