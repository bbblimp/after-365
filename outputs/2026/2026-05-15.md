## 1. Federal Reserve opens a public rethink of its monetary-policy framework

**Original date:** 2025-05-15
**Type:** Economics / Central Banking
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-15, Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell used opening remarks at a framework-review conference to say officials needed to reconsider language around employment shortfalls and average inflation targeting. The remarks treated the post-pandemic inflation shock and a possible era of more frequent supply shocks as reasons to revisit the 2020 policy framework.

### State after 365 days
Within 365 days, the review had produced a revised FOMC Statement on Longer-Run Goals and Monetary Policy Strategy. On 2025-08-22, the Fed unanimously approved updates that kept the 2 percent longer-run inflation goal but moved the strategy away from the distinctive 2020 language around employment shortfalls and average inflation overshoots.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was procedural but important: the May conference made the review's likely direction public, and the August statement became the formal policy framework guiding FOMC communication and future decisions.

### Indirect impacts
The change marked a partial institutional lesson from the 2021-2022 inflation episode. It signaled that the Fed wanted a framework more robust to supply shocks and less tilted toward tolerating inflation overshoots after periods of low inflation.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could identify the likely reconsideration, but it could not yet know the exact text adopted in August or that the Committee would approve the revised statement unanimously.

### One-year assessment
The May 15 remarks were consequential because they were an early public marker of the Fed's turn from the 2020 framework toward a more symmetric treatment of employment and inflation risks. The result was not a change in the inflation target, but it did change the official language through which future monetary-policy tradeoffs would be explained.

## 2. Libya accepts ICC jurisdiction over alleged crimes from 2011 through 2027

**Original date:** 2025-05-15
**Type:** International Justice / Accountability
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-15, the International Criminal Court said its Registrar had received Libya's declaration accepting ICC jurisdiction over alleged crimes committed on Libyan territory from 2011 through the end of 2027. The declaration expanded the formal cooperation framework at a time when Libya remained divided and armed groups retained substantial power.

### State after 365 days
A year later, the declaration had become part of live litigation and advocacy around Libya-related ICC cases. Human Rights Watch noted in May 2026 that a Libyan suspect before the Court was challenging the validity of the 2025 declaration, while the prosecutor's office had already cited Libya's acceptance as a basis for continuing priority investigations beyond May 2026.

### Direct impacts
The direct legal effect was to give the ICC an express Libyan declaration covering alleged Rome Statute crimes on Libyan territory across a long period, from the 2011 uprising through the end of 2027.

### Indirect impacts
The declaration strengthened international accountability arguments but did not resolve the central enforcement problem: whether Libyan authorities and armed groups would cooperate with arrests, evidence access, victim participation, and witness protection.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports could mark the declaration as a legal opening, but they could not know how quickly defense challenges and continuing cooperation disputes would test its practical value.

### One-year assessment
The acceptance of jurisdiction was a meaningful accountability development, but not a guarantee of accountability. After 365 days, its importance lay in creating a legal pathway that prosecutors and victims could invoke, while also exposing how dependent international justice remains on domestic cooperation in a fragmented state.

## 3. The May 15-16 tornado outbreak becomes a deadly central U.S. disaster

**Original date:** 2025-05-15
**Type:** Weather / Disaster Risk
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-15, the first storms in a central U.S. severe-weather sequence developed, with the most destructive phase following on May 16. NOAA later described the May 15-16 episode as one of two destructive outbreaks in a week-long severe-weather pattern across the central and eastern United States.

### State after 365 days
Within days, National Weather Service surveys documented the scale of the disaster. The Somerset-London tornado in Kentucky reached EF4 intensity with estimated 170 mph winds, a maximum width of 1,700 yards, and 19 confirmed fatalities. NOAA also recorded severe impacts in Missouri, including an EF3 tornado through parts of St. Louis, and substantial regional damage and power disruption.

### Direct impacts
The direct impacts were deaths, injuries, destroyed homes and businesses, collapsed structures, road closures, power outages, and extensive emergency response across multiple states.

### Indirect impacts
The outbreak added to the public record on nocturnal tornado risk, warning reception, urban tornado exposure, and resilience needs for local power, road, airport, and emergency-management systems.

### What original coverage missed
On May 15 the full toll was not yet visible. The most consequential tornadoes came the following day, and only later surveys established path length, wind estimates, width, fatalities, and damage characteristics.

### One-year assessment
The event was not just a severe-weather episode; it became a measurable disaster with long recovery needs and a detailed public meteorological record. Its archival value lies in how a forecast severe-weather period translated into one of the year's clearest examples of high-consequence tornado exposure.

## 4. Colombia's labor-reform referendum fight turns into a congressional lawmaking test

**Original date:** 2025-05-15
**Type:** Labor Policy / Political Institutions
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-05-15, Colombia was absorbing the Senate's narrow rejection of President Gustavo Petro's proposed labor-reform referendum. Petro called supporters into the streets and demanded another vote, while lawmakers had also revived the possibility of handling the labor changes through Congress rather than a national plebiscite.

### State after 365 days
The confrontation did not end with a referendum. In June 2025, Colombia's Senate approved a modified labor reform bill, including changes on the daytime workday, weekend and holiday pay, and social security payments for delivery-app drivers. Petro then said the referendum call would be withdrawn because the reform had become law.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was to block Petro's preferred plebiscitary route and force the labor agenda back through legislative bargaining. The later bill delivered a modified version of reforms the government had sought by referendum.

### Indirect impacts
The episode tested the boundary between executive pressure, congressional procedure, street mobilization, and constitutional review in Colombia. It also made labor reform part of a wider institutional contest ahead of the 2026 political cycle.

### What original coverage missed
The immediate story looked like another legislative defeat for Petro. It missed that the same confrontation would help create pressure for a negotiated congressional reform within weeks.

### One-year assessment
The referendum fight was consequential because it converted a blocked populist appeal into a narrower but concrete labor-law outcome. The durable lesson is institutional: the reform survived, but only after being pulled back into Congress and court-supervised constitutional limits.
