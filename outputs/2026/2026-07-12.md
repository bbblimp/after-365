## 1. New Caledonia's Bougival accord becomes a fragile autonomy test

**Original date:** 2025-07-12
**Type:** Constitutional Politics / Decolonization / Pacific
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-07-12, French officials and New Caledonian pro- and anti-independence leaders reached the Bougival agreement after extended talks near Paris. The proposal would create a sui generis State of New Caledonia within the French Republic, add a New Caledonian nationality alongside French nationality, and set a path for further transfers of powers if later approved.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the agreement looks less like a settled constitutional endpoint than a fragile framework. The FLNKS rejected the deal in August 2025, later implementation depended on French constitutional legislation, and by 2026 reporting and reference records showed the legal timetable disrupted by parliamentary and political resistance.

### Direct impacts
The accord temporarily gave Paris, loyalist parties, and some independence actors a common text after the violent 2024 crisis. It also moved the debate from a binary independence referendum toward staged autonomy, nationality, electoral-roll rules, and possible future sovereign-power transfers.

### Indirect impacts
The compromise exposed fractures inside the independence movement and kept New Caledonia's nickel economy, voting rules, and strategic Pacific status tied to French national politics. Its uncertain implementation showed how dependent local settlement remained on parliamentary arithmetic in Paris as well as consent in the territory.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could describe the institutional design and the relief of reaching an agreement, but it could not know whether the text would survive approval by the principal independence bloc, French constitutional procedure, and local electoral deadlines.

### One-year assessment
The Bougival accord was consequential because it offered a serious post-riot path short of full independence. One year later, its value is mostly as a stress test: it clarified the terms of possible shared sovereignty, but it did not by itself resolve New Caledonia's legitimacy problem.

## 2. The Ituri ADF massacre shows the limits of Congo's parallel peace tracks

**Original date:** 2025-07-12
**Type:** Armed Conflict / Civilian Harm / Islamist Insurgency
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-07-12, the Associated Press reported that Allied Democratic Forces fighters aligned with the Islamic State had killed 66 civilians in villages in the Walese Vonkutu chiefdom of Ituri province. A UN mission spokesperson described the killings as a bloodbath, and the victims were reported killed with machetes after recent Congolese-Ugandan operations against the group.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the attack reads as part of a persistent ADF campaign rather than a contained episode. Subsequent attacks in Ituri and North Kivu, including later mass killings and a church attack in Komanda, showed that joint military pressure had not eliminated the group's ability to strike civilians.

### Direct impacts
The immediate impact was the killing of dozens of civilians, renewed fear in rural communities near the Uganda border, and evidence that military operations could be followed by retaliatory violence against people with little protection.

### Indirect impacts
The massacre complicated the wider diplomatic picture in eastern Congo. The same period included U.S.- and Qatar-backed efforts around Rwanda, Congo, and M23, but the ADF violence showed that even progress on one armed front would leave major civilian-security failures unresolved.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports could identify the toll and likely retaliation motive, but not the durability of the pattern. Follow-up attacks showed that the ADF problem was not absorbed by the better-known M23 peace track and could continue to destabilize Ituri independently.

### One-year assessment
The July 2025 Ituri killings were consequential because they marked a sharp civilian-cost warning inside a crowded conflict system. One year later, the central lesson is that eastern Congo's peace efforts remained partial when they addressed one armed actor while civilians faced another.

## 3. Camp Mystic flood records move a Texas disaster from rescue to accountability

**Original date:** 2025-07-12
**Type:** Disaster / Emergency Management / Youth Safety
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-07-12, as central Texas remained under flood threat after the July 4 disaster, Associated Press reporting found that FEMA had removed dozens of Camp Mystic buildings from 100-year flood maps after appeals years before the flood. The record shifted attention from the immediate search and recovery toward floodplain governance, warning systems, and camp oversight.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the accountability arc is clearer. The Hill Country flood killed more than 130 people, including 25 Camp Mystic campers and two counselors. By the first anniversary, investigations, lawsuits, bankruptcy proceedings, and new Texas camp-safety and warning-system laws had turned the disaster into a continuing regulatory case.

### Direct impacts
The July 12 records reporting gave investigators, families, and policymakers a concrete document trail for questions about why children were housed in places later shown to be vulnerable. It also sharpened scrutiny of FEMA map amendments, local warning gaps, and private camp emergency planning.

### Indirect impacts
The disaster changed the politics of youth-camp safety in Texas. New laws and local warning investments followed, but the region remained flash-flood prone, and litigation kept unresolved questions about responsibility, insurance, and institutional duty of care in public view.

### What original coverage missed
Early disaster coverage necessarily focused on missing people, rescues, and the death toll. The one-year record shows that the more durable issue was not only the storm's intensity, but the accumulated decisions about maps, siting, alerts, staffing, and evacuation authority before the water rose.

### One-year assessment
The Camp Mystic records were consequential because they converted a natural-disaster story into a preventability and governance inquiry. One year later, the disaster's legacy is measured less by memorial statements than by whether floodplain rules, warning systems, and camp oversight actually changed.
