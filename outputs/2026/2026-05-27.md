## 1. EU ministers approve the EUR 150 billion SAFE defence-loan instrument

**Original date:** 2025-05-27
**Type:** Defence / European Integration / Public Finance
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-27, EU governments approved the Security Action for Europe instrument, known as SAFE, creating up to EUR 150 billion in EU-backed loans for joint defence procurement. The Council described it as the EU's first large defence investment program at bloc level, adopted under pressure from the war in Ukraine, concerns about future Russian threats, and uncertainty over U.S. security commitments.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, SAFE is no longer only a legal instrument. The regulation entered into force on 2025-05-29, the Commission received national defence-investment plans, and by April 2026 the Council had green-lit SAFE funding for 18 member states. Council material says 15 of the 19 submitted plans included projects with Ukraine, showing that the instrument became a practical channel for common procurement rather than remaining only a headline borrowing authority.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was to make common defence borrowing available for urgent and large-scale equipment purchases, with the Commission able to raise funds and conclude loan agreements. It also moved defence procurement further into EU-level fiscal and industrial policy, even though the assistance takes the form of loans rather than grants.

### Indirect impacts
SAFE reinforced the idea that European defence readiness is now partly a budgetary-integration question. It also opened participation paths for partner countries that have security and defence partnerships with the EU, linking European rearmament to wider industrial and diplomatic relationships beyond the Union.

### What original coverage missed
Opening coverage could see the scale of the EUR 150 billion ceiling and Hungary's abstention, but it could not yet show whether member states would use the facility. The 2026 approvals show real demand, while also revealing that the program's practical effect depends on national plans, loan agreements, procurement execution, and defence-industrial capacity.

### One-year assessment
The May 27 SAFE approval was consequential because it converted a general European rearmament pledge into a funded EU mechanism. One year later, its importance lies in the institutional precedent: defence readiness became a shared borrowing and procurement problem, even though delivery of actual capabilities remains unfinished.

## 2. France's National Assembly approves an assisted-dying bill on first reading

**Original date:** 2025-05-27
**Type:** Health Law / Bioethics / Parliamentary Politics
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-27, France's National Assembly approved a bill that would create a regulated right to assisted dying for some adults with serious, incurable, life-threatening illness and severe suffering. The vote, 305 in favor and 199 against, sent the text to the Senate, where a conservative majority was expected to scrutinize or amend it.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the measure remains in the legislative process rather than in force. The Senate rejected the assisted-dying bill in January 2026, sending it back to the lower house. The National Assembly then continued the process, while the separate palliative-care track remained politically easier. The case therefore stands as an unresolved but sustained attempt to change French end-of-life law.

### Direct impacts
The vote gave the proposal formal parliamentary momentum after earlier disruption from France's 2024 dissolution and political instability. It also clarified the bill's safeguards, including limits on eligibility and a distinction between self-administration and medical assistance where a patient's condition prevents self-administration.

### Indirect impacts
The debate forced French institutions to handle end-of-life policy through ordinary parliamentary bargaining rather than only presidential promise or citizen-convention recommendation. It also separated broad support for palliative care from a more divisive assisted-dying framework.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could describe the Assembly vote as historic, but it could not know whether the Senate would absorb, amend, or block the text. The one-year record shows that the vote was an important threshold, not a final settlement.

### One-year assessment
The May 27 vote was consequential because it made assisted dying a live statutory question in France rather than a recurring ethical debate without a bill. One year later, its significance is procedural and cultural: France moved closer to legal change, but the law had not yet cleared the institutional resistance built into the bicameral process.

## 3. SpaceX's Starship Flight 9 loses control and breaks apart

**Original date:** 2025-05-27
**Type:** Space / Commercial Launch / Engineering Risk
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-27, SpaceX launched the ninth integrated Starship test flight from Starbase, Texas. The flight did not meet its main objectives: the payload door failed to open fully, planned mock Starlink deployment was canceled, the spacecraft lost attitude control during the coast phase, and it broke apart before completing a controlled Indian Ocean reentry.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, Flight 9 reads as the last failure in a run of early-2025 Starship setbacks rather than the end of the program. The FAA required a mishap investigation, later closed the Flight 9 inquiry and cleared Flight 10, and SpaceX completed more successful later flights, including the eleventh test flight in October 2025. At the same time, a May 2026 attempt with a larger Starship was halted by launch-tower issues, showing that the test program remained technically demanding.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was another regulatory and engineering pause around the vehicle loss, focused on the Starship upper stage and the failed reentry profile. It delayed the planned sequence of tests that SpaceX needs for payload deployment, reentry control, and eventual reuse.

### Indirect impacts
Because NASA depends on Starship-derived systems for planned lunar landing architecture, repeated test failures had consequences beyond SpaceX's Mars ambitions. The later recovery also showed the program's iterative pattern: failures can feed corrective actions and return-to-flight approvals, but they do not eliminate schedule risk.

### What original coverage missed
Same-day coverage could identify the loss of vehicle and missed objectives, but it could not know whether Flight 9 would become a lasting program crisis or a recoverable test failure. The one-year record supports the latter interpretation, with important caveats about ongoing launch-system complexity.

### One-year assessment
Flight 9 was consequential because it exposed unresolved reliability problems in a vehicle central to U.S. commercial spaceflight and NASA's lunar plans. One year later, it looks less like a standalone disaster than a visible data point in a rapid, failure-tolerant development campaign whose operational promises still require proof.

## 4. A federal judge blocks retaliation against New York's congestion-pricing program

**Original date:** 2025-05-27
**Type:** Urban Policy / Transportation / Federalism
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-05-27, U.S. District Judge Lewis Liman temporarily blocked the Trump administration from withholding federal funds or taking other punitive action against New York over Manhattan's congestion-pricing toll. The toll had begun in January 2025 after federal approval under the prior administration, but the new Transportation Department sought to rescind that approval.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the temporary order proved to be more than a short delay. In March 2026, Liman ruled that the federal government lacked authority to unilaterally rescind approval of the program. Follow-up reporting citing MTA data said the first year brought roughly 27 million fewer vehicles entering Manhattan's core, faster commutes for drivers who paid the fee, and more than USD 550 million in transit revenue.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was to keep the toll operating while the lawsuit proceeded and to prevent the federal government from using unrelated project approvals or funds as leverage. That preserved a revenue stream for the MTA during the litigation.

### Indirect impacts
The case became a test of whether a change in presidential administration could reverse a federally approved, already operating state transportation program. It also gave the first U.S. congestion-pricing system enough time to produce measurable data rather than being judged only through forecasts and political claims.

### What original coverage missed
Original coverage could identify the temporary restraining order and the administration's pressure tactics, but it could not know whether the program would survive final review or show practical results. The later ruling and first-year data made the May 27 order look like a bridge to durability.

### One-year assessment
The May 27 order was consequential because it protected an unusually visible urban-policy experiment at the moment federal opposition could have stopped it. One year later, congestion pricing remained contested, but the legal and empirical terrain had shifted: opponents still had political arguments, while the program had court protection, revenue, and operating data.
