## 1. The U.S. Senate passes the GENIUS Act stablecoin bill

**Original date:** 2025-06-17
**Type:** Financial Regulation / Digital Assets / Legislative Process
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-17, the U.S. Senate passed S. 1582, the GENIUS Act, by a 68-30 vote. The bill proposed a federal framework for payment stablecoins, including rules for permitted issuers, reserve backing, and federal or state supervision.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the Senate vote reads as the decisive break point for the first major federal crypto-market statute. Congress.gov records that the House passed the bill on 2025-07-17 and that it became Public Law 119-27 on 2025-07-18. The stablecoin framework also became part of a wider policy debate over crypto market structure, bank-like payment instruments, reserve assets, and the role of dollar-backed tokens in financial markets.

### Direct impacts
The vote moved stablecoin regulation from a negotiated Senate proposal to a bill with bipartisan chamber approval. It gave the House a Senate-passed vehicle rather than a discussion draft and created the timetable that led to enactment one month later.

### Indirect impacts
The bill's progress signaled that parts of the crypto industry had moved from defensive regulation fights into affirmative federal legislation. It also connected digital-asset policy to banking supervision, Treasury-market demand, consumer-protection debates, and competition between bank and nonbank payment issuers.

### What original coverage missed
Same-day coverage could identify the Senate breakthrough and the coming House fight, but it could not yet show that the bill would pass the House largely intact and become law on 2025-07-18.

### One-year assessment
The June 17 vote was consequential because it turned stablecoin regulation into an enactable federal project. One year later, its significance lies less in the roll-call total alone than in its role as the legislative hinge for Public Law 119-27 and for the normalization of payment stablecoins within U.S. financial regulation.

## 2. British MPs vote to decriminalise self-managed abortion for women in England and Wales

**Original date:** 2025-06-17
**Type:** Reproductive Rights / Criminal Law / Parliamentary Process
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-17, MPs in the House of Commons voted 379-137 for an amendment to the Crime and Policing Bill that would prevent women in England and Wales from being criminally prosecuted for ending their own pregnancies outside the existing abortion framework. The vote left the ordinary abortion access framework, including medical regulation and time limits, formally in place.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the vote had remained a major reproductive-rights and criminal-law marker rather than a symbolic division. Follow-up coverage in 2026 showed the measure moving through Lords scrutiny, with peers considering attempts to remove or alter the decriminalisation clause and with related provisions to pardon women convicted or investigated under old abortion offences.

### Direct impacts
The Commons vote inserted a major abortion-law reform into a government crime bill and showed a large parliamentary majority for ending prosecutions of women who self-manage abortions. It did not by itself remove all abortion regulation or alter clinical rules for providers.

### Indirect impacts
The vote shifted the policy frame from access alone to the criminal exposure created by nineteenth- and early twentieth-century offences. It also reflected the political effect of recent investigations and prosecutions of women after suspected unlawful abortions, miscarriages, or stillbirths.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage could not know how the Lords would handle the clause or that related pardon and record-expungement provisions would become part of the follow-up debate.

### One-year assessment
The vote mattered because it separated women seeking or self-managing abortions from the criminal-law framework that had remained beneath modern clinical access. One year later, the unresolved details were still important, but the Commons division had already become the central procedural event in the most significant England-and-Wales abortion-law reform effort in decades.

## 3. SAMHSA announces the end of 988's LGBTQ youth Press 3 service

**Original date:** 2025-06-17
**Type:** Public Health / Mental Health / Civil Rights
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-17, the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration announced that the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline's specialized LGBTQ youth Press 3 service would end on 2025-07-17. The service had been created to route LGBTQ young people to counselors with specialized training, and contemporary reports said it had handled roughly 1.3 million contacts since 2022.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the decision had become more than a budget line. The Press 3 option did shut down in July 2025, The Trevor Project and other providers absorbed more pressure outside the federal 988 subnetwork, and 2026 reporting described a congressional directive and administration work toward restoring LGBTQ youth crisis support while reconciling that effort with the administration's broader sex-and-gender executive orders.

### Direct impacts
The announcement gave providers, callers, advocates, and states one month of notice before the specialized federal routing option ended. The general 988 Lifeline remained, but the dedicated LGBTQ youth subnetwork was removed from the federal hotline structure.

### Indirect impacts
The decision turned suicide-prevention infrastructure into a visible civil-rights and public-health fight. It also increased reliance on nonprofit crisis lines and private fundraising, especially for The Trevor Project, and forced Congress and states to consider whether targeted crisis support should be protected separately from general hotline funding.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports could identify the planned shutdown and the backlash, but they could not yet show the operational consequences after July 2025 or the later effort to restore a specialized service through the 2026 budget process.

### One-year assessment
The Press 3 decision was consequential because it removed a targeted federal crisis-support pathway for a population with elevated suicide risk. One year later, the attempted restoration underscored the same point: the core issue was not only hotline branding, but whether national crisis infrastructure should include specialized access for vulnerable groups.
