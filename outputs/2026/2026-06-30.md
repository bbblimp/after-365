## 1. Canada rescinds its digital services tax before first collections

**Original date:** 2025-06-30
**Type:** Trade / Digital Taxation / U.S.-Canada Relations
**Confidence:** High

### Then
Canada halted the June 30 collection of its digital services tax and said legislation would follow to rescind the tax. The decision came after the United States suspended trade talks over a levy that would have applied to large digital-service firms, many of them American, on Canadian-source revenue.

### State after 365 days
A year later, the move reads less as a settled tax-policy resolution than as an early example of how Canada managed U.S. tariff pressure under Prime Minister Mark Carney. Platform surcharges linked to the tax were unwound, but Canada-U.S. trade relations remained exposed to repeated tariff and negotiation shocks rather than moving cleanly to a durable settlement.

### Direct impacts
The immediate collection was stopped, the planned retroactive bill to large technology companies was avoided, and bilateral talks resumed toward a July 2025 target date. Canadian businesses that had faced pass-through digital advertising or marketplace fees received at least partial relief as platforms adjusted their charges.

### Indirect impacts
The reversal weakened Canada's unilateral digital-tax position and reinforced a broader lesson for mid-sized economies: digital taxation can be difficult to sustain when it collides with a larger trading partner's tariff threats. It also kept attention on unresolved multilateral tax talks rather than establishing a stable national model.

### What original coverage missed
Immediate coverage emphasized the drama of the climbdown and the revived talks. The more durable issue was institutional: Canada had spent years building a tax tool that could be discarded within days when broader trade exposure became the binding constraint.

### One-year assessment
The rescission was consequential mainly as leverage politics. It protected negotiations and reduced near-term costs, but it did not solve the larger problem of how to tax digital activity while preserving room to maneuver in an unstable North American trade environment.

## 2. China partly lifts its ban on Japanese seafood imports

**Original date:** 2025-06-30
**Type:** Trade / Food Safety / Northeast Asia Diplomacy
**Confidence:** High

### Then
China reopened its market to seafood from much of Japan after a nearly two-year ban imposed over the release of treated wastewater from Fukushima Daiichi. The reopening was conditional: seafood from Fukushima and nine nearby prefectures remained barred, and exporters still had to satisfy Chinese registration and certification requirements.

### State after 365 days
By November 2025, Japan had resumed seafood shipments to China, starting with Hokkaido scallops. The recovery remained partial, and later China-Japan tensions over Taiwan showed that the seafood channel could again become a diplomatic instrument rather than a purely technical food-safety matter.

### Direct impacts
The decision restored a path into what had been Japan's largest overseas seafood market before the 2023 ban. It offered particular relief to scallop and sea cucumber exporters, though the practical restart depended on re-registration and Chinese monitoring.

### Indirect impacts
The episode showed how Fukushima wastewater concerns, food safety, and Northeast Asian security politics were intertwined. Scientific sampling helped create room for the June decision, but it did not insulate trade from later diplomatic retaliation.

### What original coverage missed
The reopening looked like a normalization step, but the remaining prefectural bans and certification hurdles meant that the commercial recovery would be gradual. The later re-politicization of seafood trade was a reminder that technical assurances had only limited diplomatic force.

### One-year assessment
The June 2025 decision mattered, but as a conditional thaw rather than a durable settlement. Its value was real for Japanese exporters, yet the one-year record suggests the market remained hostage to broader China-Japan relations.

## 3. Deadly Gaza aid-site shootings deepen scrutiny of the Gaza Humanitarian Foundation model

**Original date:** 2025-06-30
**Type:** Humanitarian Aid / War / Civilian Protection
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
Hospitals, witnesses, and Gaza authorities reported more deaths among Palestinians seeking food near aid-distribution points, part of a wider June pattern around the Gaza Humanitarian Foundation system. Israel disputed or qualified many accounts of incidents near aid sites, while the United Nations and aid organizations increasingly warned that the distribution model was unsafe and inconsistent with humanitarian norms.

### State after 365 days
The model did not become a stable replacement for the U.N.-centered aid system. After the October 2025 ceasefire, the Gaza Humanitarian Foundation suspended operations and later announced that it would end its Gaza mission, while critics pointed to the deaths and injuries around distribution points as evidence that the system had failed basic civilian-protection tests.

### Direct impacts
The immediate effect was further loss of civilian life and intensified international pressure over how food was being distributed in Gaza. The incidents also strengthened demands from humanitarian groups to close the GHF system and return to established aid channels.

### Indirect impacts
The controversy changed the policy debate from whether private distribution could bypass Hamas to whether militarized and centralized aid access was itself producing unacceptable risk. It also left a record likely to shape future legal and humanitarian reviews of aid delivery in active war zones.

### What original coverage missed
Daily casualty reports captured the severity of the violence, but the larger institutional failure became clearer only later: a system promoted as an alternative aid model could not maintain legitimacy with much of the humanitarian sector or avoid repeated deadly incidents near its access points.

### One-year assessment
The June 30 incidents were part of a broader pattern rather than an isolated turning point. Their one-year significance lies in documenting how aid architecture can become a civilian-protection problem when access, security, and political objectives are fused.

## 4. The Supreme Court agrees to hear Cox's ISP copyright-liability case

**Original date:** 2025-06-30
**Type:** Technology Law / Copyright / Internet Infrastructure
**Confidence:** High

### Then
The Supreme Court agreed to hear Cox Communications' challenge in a copyright case brought by major record labels over subscriber music piracy. The case asked how far internet service providers can be held responsible when customers use general internet access to infringe copyrights.

### State after 365 days
On March 25, 2026, the Court unanimously sided with Cox, holding that an ISP is not contributorily liable merely because it provides general internet access with knowledge that some users may infringe. The decision reversed lower-court liability findings and sharply limited one path for copyright owners to pressure ISPs into terminating users.

### Direct impacts
Cox avoided contributory copyright liability in the Supreme Court, and the ruling gave broadband providers stronger protection against being treated as copyright enforcement agents for customer activity. Record labels lost a major test case built around repeat-infringer notices and account termination.

### Indirect impacts
The ruling affects more than music piracy. It clarifies secondary-liability standards for general-purpose internet services and reduces the risk that schools, households, businesses, or shared networks could lose access based on accusations against individual users.

### What original coverage missed
When certiorari was granted, coverage focused on the billion-dollar verdict and music-industry stakes. The eventual decision showed the Court was also concerned with administrability and the social cost of turning network access providers into gatekeepers.

### One-year assessment
The grant became a consequential technology-law case. It did not end copyright enforcement online, but it narrowed a liability theory that could have made basic internet access more conditional on private copyright policing.
