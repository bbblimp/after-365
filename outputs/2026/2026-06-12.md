## 1. Air India Flight 171 crashes after takeoff from Ahmedabad

**Original date:** 2025-06-12
**Type:** Aviation Safety / Accident Investigation / India
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-12, Air India Flight 171, a Boeing 787-8 bound from Ahmedabad to London Gatwick, crashed shortly after takeoff into the B. J. Medical College complex. Early reports described a major mass-casualty accident involving 242 people on board, international passengers, and deaths on the ground.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the confirmed toll stands at 260 deaths: 241 people on board and 19 on the ground, with one passenger surviving. A preliminary investigation reported that both engine fuel-control switches moved from RUN to CUTOFF seconds after liftoff, but the final causal finding had not been published by the anniversary and families were still seeking clearer answers.

### Direct impacts
The crash destroyed the aircraft, killed passengers, crew, medical students and others on the ground, halted normal operations at Ahmedabad airport, and drew investigation support from India, the United Kingdom, the United States, Boeing, and engine maker GE Aerospace.

### Indirect impacts
The accident became the first fatal crash and hull loss involving the Boeing 787, prompted enhanced checks of Air India's 787 fleet, retired the AI171 flight number, and renewed scrutiny of how accident investigations communicate preliminary findings without assigning blame prematurely.

### What original coverage missed
First-day coverage could not know the final victim identification process, the cockpit-recorder detail about fuel-control switches, or the persistence of unanswered questions one year later. It also could not yet show the strain on the sole survivor and victims' families as compensation and legal processes developed.

### One-year assessment
The crash remains consequential as both a human disaster and an aviation-safety case. Its long-term meaning depends on the final investigation, but the year since has already shown that the central issue is not only what failed in the final seconds, but whether the explanation is strong enough to restore confidence and prevent recurrence.

## 2. Flash flooding in San Antonio sweeps vehicles into Beitel Creek

**Original date:** 2025-06-12
**Type:** Extreme Weather / Urban Flood Risk / Public Safety
**Confidence:** High

### Then
On 2025-06-12, intense rainfall in San Antonio rapidly flooded roads, swept vehicles into creeks, and forced dozens of water rescues. Early reporting placed the confirmed death toll at five with others missing, centered on the northeast side of the city where vehicles were carried from roadways by fast-rising water.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the flood is understood as a 13-death event, with 11 people killed near Beitel Creek after vehicles were swept from the Loop 410 access road and two others killed elsewhere in the city. Later review focused on why the road had no warning system or closure in place and how vegetation, drainage, modeling, and interagency coordination affected the flood hazard.

### Direct impacts
The flood killed 13 people, damaged vehicles and infrastructure, required more than 70 rescues, and left San Antonio officials reviewing emergency warnings, road closures, and responsibility for flood-prone corridors.

### Indirect impacts
The event became a local case study in urban flash-flood risk: a dangerous combination of intense rainfall, impervious surfaces, roadway exposure in a floodplain, and warning systems that did not match the actual hazard experienced by drivers before dawn.

### What original coverage missed
Initial reports captured the rescues and rising toll but could not yet identify all victims or evaluate whether the affected access road had been recognized as a high-risk location. Later engineering review added detail about vegetation, water levels, flood modeling, and proposed warning-system changes.

### One-year assessment
The San Antonio flood mattered beyond its immediate death toll because it exposed a gap between mapped or expected risk and lived risk on a major urban roadway. One year later, the durable lesson is practical: flood planning has to reach the driver before the water reaches the road.

## 3. Micron announces a $200 billion U.S. memory-chip investment

**Original date:** 2025-06-12
**Type:** Semiconductors / Industrial Policy / AI Supply Chains
**Confidence:** Medium

### Then
On 2025-06-12, the U.S. Commerce Department announced that Micron planned to invest about $200 billion in U.S. semiconductor manufacturing and research and development. The plan covered Idaho, New York and Virginia, including advanced memory production, high-bandwidth-memory packaging, and additional CHIPS Act support.

### State after 365 days
After 365 days, the announcement has remained an important marker in U.S. efforts to localize advanced memory supply, especially for AI and defense-linked demand. The follow-through is still long-horizon: Idaho was being prioritized, New York timelines extended into the 2030s and 2040s, and the practical impact depends on permitting, labor, equipment, demand cycles, and federal funding administration.

### Direct impacts
The direct impact was to expand Micron's public U.S. investment plan, add a second Boise fabrication facility, modernize Manassas, add high-bandwidth-memory packaging ambitions, and attach incremental federal support to a much larger private-capital commitment.

### Indirect impacts
The announcement reinforced the shift from generic chip reshoring to a more specific concern: memory chips and HBM packaging as strategic inputs for AI, automotive, industrial, aerospace and defense supply chains. It also increased pressure on local infrastructure, labor pipelines and environmental review in host states.

### What original coverage missed
Launch-day coverage could emphasize the dollar figure and job estimates, but it could not yet show the sequencing tradeoffs. Later reporting suggested that the real story was not a single buildout, but prioritization among Idaho, New York and Virginia over a multi-decade schedule.

### One-year assessment
Micron's announcement was consequential as industrial policy, but not yet as completed industrial capacity. One year later, it is best read as a large, traceable commitment whose significance will be measured by fabs actually built, HBM capacity actually onshored, and whether U.S. memory production becomes less dependent on East Asia.
